\chapter{Why anyone studies heat in the Moon}

The Moon looks dead and unchanging, but just beneath its surface there is
a slow, quiet flow of heat --- and that flow carries information we can
get no other way.

\paragraph{Surface drama, deep calm.}
The Moon's surface is roasted by the Sun through its two-week day and
freezes through its two-week night, swinging by roughly
$250\,^\circ$C. But a single metre down, none of that is felt. Down
there the temperature is set by only two things: how well the soil
conducts heat, and a faint, steady warmth seeping up from the Moon's
interior.

\paragraph{The number we are chasing.}
The key property of the soil is its \textbf{thermal conductivity} ---
how easily heat passes through it. We write it $K$, and its value deep
down (where the soil is compacted and settled) is $K_d$. Lunar soil is
an extraordinary insulator, which is exactly why the surface heat cannot
reach far below.

\paragraph{Why it matters.}
Today every lunar thermal model uses \emph{one single value of $K_d$ for
the whole Moon}, never checked against direct underground data --- because
such data exist at only two places in history: the Apollo 15 and 17
landing sites, where astronauts buried thermometers in 1971--72.

\begin{keybox}
We use the only two underground temperature records ever taken on the
Moon to test a hidden, universal assumption: is one conductivity value
good enough for the entire Moon? We will build, step by step, to the
answer: no --- the two sites differ by about a factor of two, and the
difference is real.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Heat, temperature, and the law of conduction}

\section{What heat and temperature actually are}
Everything is made of atoms, and atoms are always jiggling. \textbf{Temperature}
measures the \emph{average} jiggle: hot means vigorous jiggling, cold
means sluggish. \textbf{Heat} is the \emph{energy} of that jiggling on
the move --- energy flowing from a more-jiggly place to a less-jiggly
one. We measure temperature in kelvin (K); $0\,$K is no jiggling at all,
and lunar deep soil sits around $250\,$K ($-23\,^\circ$C).

\section{Conduction: heat seeping through solid stuff}
When a hot region touches a cold region, the fast atoms jostle their
slower neighbours, passing energy along. This is \textbf{conduction}
(Figure~\ref{fig:conduction}). No material moves --- only energy does,
handed atom to atom. It is how a metal spoon's handle warms in hot soup.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_conduction.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Conduction.} Heat always flows from hot to cold. How
fast depends on two things: how good a conductor the material is ($K$),
and how steep the temperature change is. A steeper change, or a better
conductor, moves more heat per second.}
\label{fig:conduction}
\end{figure}

\section{Two words we need: flux and gradient}
\textbf{Heat flux} ($q$) is how much heat energy crosses one square
metre each second --- measured in watts per square metre
(W\,m$^{-2}$). \textbf{Temperature gradient} is how fast temperature
changes with distance --- here, with depth $z$. If the temperature rises
2 degrees over half a metre, the gradient is $4\,$K per metre. We write
it $\partial T/\partial z$, which is just mathematical shorthand for
``the slope of the temperature-versus-depth line.''

\section{Fourier's law: the rate of heat flow}
Two centuries ago Joseph Fourier wrote down the rule connecting these:
heat flux equals conductivity times gradient.

\begin{mathbox}
\[
q \;=\; -\,K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}
\]
\textbf{$q$} --- heat flux (W\,m$^{-2}$). \textbf{$K$} --- conductivity
(W\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$): a big $K$ passes heat easily. \textbf{$\partial
T/\partial z$} --- the temperature gradient (K\,m$^{-1}$). The minus sign
means heat flows \emph{down} the gradient, from hot toward cold.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{plainbox}
A crowded room empties through a door faster if the door is wide (big
$K$) and if it is far more crowded inside than out (steep gradient). Heat
is the crowd; Fourier's law is the turnstile count.
\end{plainbox}

\begin{keybox}
Conductivity $K$ is the one material property that turns a temperature
difference into a rate of heat flow. The entire study is an effort to
measure $K$ deep in the lunar soil.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{The materials: turning physics into numbers and code}

Fourier's law needs three material properties: the conductivity $K$, and
--- once things change in time --- the density $\rho$ and specific heat
$c_p$. This chapter explains each physically, then shows the exact code
that computes it. This is our first ``physics, then code'' pairing.

\section{Conductivity that grows with depth and temperature}
Two physical facts shape lunar conductivity:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Depth.} The surface dust is fluffy and loosely packed (a
      poor conductor); deeper down it is compressed, so grains touch
      better and conduct better. So $K$ rises from a small surface value
      $K_s$ to a larger deep value $K_d$ over a short transition depth
      $H$.
\item \textbf{Temperature.} Heat also crosses the tiny gaps between
      grains as infrared light (radiation). Radiation grows fast with
      temperature (as $T^3$), so warm soil conducts a bit better. This
      is the ``radiative term,'' controlled by a coefficient $\chi$.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{mathbox}
\[
K(T,z) \;=\; \underbrace{\big[\,K_d - (K_d-K_s)\,e^{-z/H}\,\big]}_{\text{grows with depth}}
\;\times\;
\underbrace{\big[\,1 + \chi\,(T/350)^3\,\big]}_{\text{grows with temperature}}
\]
The first bracket starts at $K_s$ at the surface ($z=0$, where
$e^{0}=1$) and climbs toward $K_d$ deep down (where $e^{-z/H}\to0$). The
second bracket is 1 plus the radiative boost: $\chi$ sets its strength
and 350\,K is just a reference temperature. $K_d$ is the single number we
will retrieve.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{plainbox}
Conductivity is not one number but a recipe: ``start small at the dusty
top, rise to $K_d$ once packed, and add a little extra when warm.'' Our
job is to find the one ingredient $K_d$ that makes this recipe match the
buried thermometers.
\end{plainbox}

Here is exactly that formula in code. Notice how each line matches a
piece of the equation above.

\codefile{lunar/properties.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def conductivity_hayne(T, z, Ks=K_SURFACE, Kd=K_DEEP,
                       H=H_PARAMETER, chi=CHI_RADIATIVE):
    T = np.asarray(T, dtype=np.float64)
    z = np.asarray(z, dtype=np.float64)
    Kc = Kd - (Kd - Ks) * np.exp(-z / H)          # grows with depth
    return Kc * (1.0 + chi * (T / T_REFERENCE)**3) # times radiative boost
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
\texttt{Kc = Kd - (Kd - Ks)*np.exp(-z/H)} is the first bracket exactly:
\texttt{np.exp(-z/H)} is $e^{-z/H}$. The last line multiplies by
\texttt{(1.0 + chi*(T/T\_REFERENCE)**3)}, the radiative bracket, where
\texttt{**3} means ``cubed'' and \texttt{T\_REFERENCE} is 350. The
inputs \texttt{T} and \texttt{z} can be whole arrays (every depth at
once), which is why the code wraps them in \texttt{np.asarray} --- NumPy
then does the arithmetic on every layer in one shot.
\end{codebox}

\section{Density and specific heat: how much heat the soil stores}
When temperatures change in time, we also need to know how much energy it
takes to warm the soil. That is set by its \textbf{density} $\rho$ (how
much mass per cubic metre) and its \textbf{specific heat} $c_p$ (energy
to warm one kilogram by one degree). Density, like conductivity, rises
with depth as the soil compacts:

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\rho(z) \;=\; \rho_d - (\rho_d - \rho_s)\,e^{-z/H}
\]
Same shape as the conductivity's depth part: surface density $\rho_s$
($\approx1100$) climbing to deep density $\rho_d$ ($\approx1800$\,kg\,m$^{-3}$)
over the depth scale $H$.
\end{mathbox}

\codefile{lunar/properties.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def density_hayne(z, rho_s=RHO_SURFACE, rho_d=RHO_DEEP, H=H_PARAMETER):
    z = np.asarray(z, dtype=np.float64)
    return rho_d - (rho_d - rho_s) * np.exp(-z / H)
\end{lstlisting}

The specific heat depends on temperature (cold soil stores heat
differently from warm soil) and is captured by a polynomial fit to
laboratory measurements of Apollo samples:

\codefile{lunar/properties.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def _cp_hayne(T):                  # c_p(T), a 4th-order polynomial
    T = np.asarray(T, dtype=np.float64)
    return (CP_HAYNE_C0 + CP_HAYNE_C1*T + CP_HAYNE_C2*T**2
            + CP_HAYNE_C3*T**3 + CP_HAYNE_C4*T**4)
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
This is just $c_0 + c_1 T + c_2 T^2 + c_3 T^3 + c_4 T^4$ --- five fixed
numbers ($c_0\ldots c_4$) multiplied by powers of temperature and added
up. The numbers themselves are measured constants.
\end{codebox}

\section{Where the numbers come from}
A rule of this project is that \emph{no number is invented} --- every
constant is stored in one file with its literature source attached. For
example:

\codefile{lunar/constants.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
#: Surface contact conductivity [W m^-1 K^-1] -- Hayne et al. (2017).
K_SURFACE: float = 7.4e-4
#: Deep contact conductivity [W m^-1 K^-1] -- Hayne et al. (2017).
K_DEEP: float = 3.4e-3          # the global value we test against
#: H-parameter (density/conductivity scale height) [m] -- Hayne (2017).
H_PARAMETER: float = 0.06
#: Stefan-Boltzmann constant [W m^-2 K^-4] -- CODATA 2018.
SIGMA_SB: float = 5.670374419e-8
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{keybox}
The physics enters the computer as three small functions ---
\texttt{conductivity\_hayne}, \texttt{density\_hayne}, \texttt{specific\_heat}
--- each a direct transcription of a formula, fed by cited constants. If
you understand the three formulas, you understand every material input to
the model.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{The heat equation: bookkeeping for energy}

Fourier's law gives the flow through one surface. To follow the
temperature \emph{everywhere, over time}, we add one idea: \textbf{energy
bookkeeping}. Take a thin slice of soil. The heat energy that accumulates
inside it per second equals what flows in from above minus what flows out
below. Warm it if more comes in than leaves; cool it if the reverse.

Apply Fourier's law to both faces of the slice and do the bookkeeping,
and you get the \textbf{heat equation} --- the master equation the whole
program solves.

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\underbrace{\rho\,c_p\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}}
  _{\text{rate heat is stored in the slice}}
\;=\;
\underbrace{\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\!\left(K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right)}
  _{\text{heat flowing in, minus out}}
\]
\textbf{Left:} how fast the slice warms ($\partial T/\partial t$ is
temperature change per second) times how much heat that costs ($\rho
c_p$). \textbf{Right:} the net conduction --- it is Fourier's flux
$K\,\partial T/\partial z$, differenced across the slice (the outer
$\partial/\partial z$). Balanced (right $=$ 0) means steady: no warming.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{plainbox}
``Temperature here changes because more heat arrives than leaves.'' That
single sentence is the heat equation. Everything the computer does is
apply it to thousands of thin slices, again and again, as the lunar day
passes.
\end{plainbox}

This equation cannot be solved with pencil and paper for a real,
property-varying column, so we solve it numerically --- the subject of
Part~II.

\chapter{The lunar column: forcing, skin, and the steady zone}

\section{Heated from both ends}
Our column of soil (Figure~\ref{fig:heatflow}a) is driven from
\emph{above} by sunlight (and night-time cooling) and from \emph{below}
by a steady warmth from the interior --- the \textbf{basal heat flux}
$Q_b$, a few thousandths of a watt per square metre.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_primer_heatflow.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The lunar soil column.} (a) Sunlight heats from above;
$Q_b$ rises from below. The top zone (coral) feels the day--night swing;
the deep zone (teal) is steady. Squares mark the buried thermometers.
(b) The daily swing collapses with depth --- below the noise by 80\,cm,
which is why deep sensors read a steady value.}
\label{fig:heatflow}
\end{figure}

\section{The skin depth: why the daily swing dies out}
The day--night wave is absorbed and smeared by each layer it passes, so
it shrinks fast with depth (Figure~\ref{fig:heatflow}b). The depth over
which it fades to nothing is the \textbf{skin depth} --- only a few
centimetres here. By 80\,cm down, the swing is smaller than any
thermometer can read.

\begin{plainbox}
Press a warm hand on a thick quilt: the surface warms, a finger-width in,
nothing. The Moon's daily heating is that hand --- it reaches only the
top few centimetres. Everything deeper feels just the steady average.
\end{plainbox}

\section{The steady deep zone and the gradient law}
Below the skin depth the soil is in \textbf{steady state}: its
temperature no longer wobbles, it only climbs slowly with depth as $Q_b$
trickles up. There the time term of the heat equation vanishes and it
collapses to the simple law that lets us measure $K_d$:

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta z} \;=\; \frac{Q_b}{K_d}
\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
K_d \;=\; \frac{Q_b}{\Delta T/\Delta z}
\]
The deep temperature climbs at a steady rate $\Delta T/\Delta z$ equal to
the flux $Q_b$ divided by the conductivity $K_d$. We \emph{measure} the
gradient from the buried thermometers and we \emph{know} $Q_b$, so we
solve for $K_d$.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{keybox}
This is the whole study in one line: read the deep temperature gradient,
divide the known heat flux by it, get the conductivity. Everything else
is doing this carefully --- with the full time-dependent model instead of
the shortcut, and with honest error bars.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Step 1 --- the depth grid}

\section{Physics: put the detail where the action is}
All the fast change happens near the surface (the skin). So instead of
slicing the column into equal layers, we make the top layers extremely
thin (2\,mm) and let each successive layer be a little thicker. This
spends computing effort where temperature changes fastest and saves it
deep down where nothing moves. (A uniform grid is actually forbidden in
this project for exactly this reason.)

\section{Code: building the grid}
\codefile{lunar/grid.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def make_geometric_grid(z_max=3.0, dz0=0.002, growth=0.15):
    faces = [0.0]
    dz = dz0
    while faces[-1] < z_max:
        faces.append(faces[-1] + dz)   # next boundary
        dz *= 1.0 + growth             # each layer thicker by `growth`
    z_face = np.asarray(faces)
    dz_arr = np.diff(z_face)                  # layer thicknesses
    z_mid  = 0.5 * (z_face[:-1] + z_face[1:]) # layer centres
    return DepthGrid(z_face=z_face, z_mid=z_mid, dz=dz_arr)
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
Start at the surface (\texttt{faces=[0.0]}) with a 2\,mm layer
(\texttt{dz0=0.002}). Each loop adds the next boundary and then grows the
thickness by 8--15\% (\texttt{dz *= 1+growth}), so layers gently fatten
with depth until the column reaches \texttt{z\_max}. \texttt{z\_face} are
the layer boundaries, \texttt{z\_mid} their centres (where temperature
lives), and \texttt{dz} their thicknesses. That is the entire scaffolding
the solver sits on.
\end{codebox}

\begin{keybox}
The continuous column becomes a stack of layers --- thin at the top,
thick at the bottom. Temperature is tracked at each layer's centre.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Step 2 --- from calculus to arithmetic}

\section{Physics/method: differences instead of derivatives}
The heat equation is written in calculus (those $\partial$ symbols mean
``rate of change''). A computer cannot do calculus, but it can do
arithmetic on our layers. The trick (the \textbf{finite-difference} or
\textbf{control-volume} method) is to replace each ``rate of change''
with a simple difference between neighbouring layers
(Figure~\ref{fig:numerical}):
\begin{itemize}
\item the gradient between two layers $\approx$ (their temperature
      difference) $\div$ (distance between their centres);
\item the heat stored in a layer $\approx$ $\rho c_p \times$ (its
      thickness) $\times$ (its temperature change).
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_numerical.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{From smooth to computable.} (a) Real temperature varies
smoothly with depth. (b) The computer represents it as a stack of thin
layers, each one number, updated step by step; fine enough layers
reproduce the smooth curve (dashed).}
\label{fig:numerical}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Conductivity at the boundary between two layers.}
Heat passing from layer $i$ to layer $i{+}1$ must cross the boundary
between them, where the two layers have different conductivities. The
physically correct combination is the \textbf{harmonic mean} (it
correctly makes the worse insulator dominate, like two resistors in
series):

\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def _face_harmonic_mean(K):           # K = conductivity at each centre
    K_face = np.empty(K.size + 1)
    for i in range(1, K.size):
        K_face[i] = 2.0 * K[i-1]*K[i] / (K[i-1] + K[i])   # harmonic mean
    return K_face
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Method: stepping forward in time (Crank--Nicolson)}
To advance temperature by one time step we use the \textbf{Crank--Nicolson}
scheme: estimate the heat flows using the \emph{average} of the situation
now and the situation one step ahead. Averaging the two makes the method
stable and accurate (using only ``now'' can blow up; using only ``next''
is less accurate). This turns one time step into a set of linked
equations --- one per layer --- where each layer's new temperature
depends on its own and its two neighbours' new temperatures.

\section{Code: assembling one time step}
The solver builds those linked equations as four lists of numbers
(\texttt{a}, \texttt{b}, \texttt{c}, \texttt{d}): \texttt{a} and
\texttt{c} are each layer's coupling to the layer above and below,
\texttt{b} is the layer itself, and \texttt{d} is the known right-hand
side from the current temperatures.

\codefile{lunar/solver.py (the heart of \_step, lightly trimmed)}
\begin{lstlisting}
K   = K_func(T_prev, grid.z_mid)        # conductivity at every layer now
rho = rho_func(grid.z_mid)              # density at every layer
cp  = cp_func(T_prev)                   # specific heat at every layer
cap = rho * cp * dz                     # heat capacity of each layer
K_face = _face_harmonic_mean(K)         # conductivity at the boundaries

# coupling of each layer to its neighbours (how easily heat crosses):
alpha_l[i] = dt * K_face[i]   / (dz_c[i]   * cap[i])   # to layer above
alpha_r[i] = dt * K_face[i+1] / (dz_c[i+1] * cap[i])   # to layer below

# the linked equation for each layer (Crank-Nicolson, theta = 0.5):
a[i] = -0.5 * alpha_l[i]                       # neighbour above
c[i] = -0.5 * alpha_r[i]                       # neighbour below
b[i] =  1.0 + 0.5*(alpha_l[i] + alpha_r[i])    # the layer itself
d[i] =  0.5*alpha_l[i]*left + (1 - 0.5*(alpha_l[i]+alpha_r[i]))*T_prev[i] \
        + 0.5*alpha_r[i]*right                 # the known part
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
Read \texttt{alpha} as ``how much heat can cross between these layers in
one time step, relative to how much heat the layer can hold'' --- big
\texttt{alpha} means strongly coupled. The \texttt{a, b, c} lines encode
the unknown new temperatures (the half, \texttt{0.5}, is the
Crank--Nicolson averaging), and \texttt{d} packages everything we already
know from the current temperatures \texttt{T\_prev}. Doing this for every
layer gives a system of linked equations to solve --- next chapter.
\end{codebox}

\begin{keybox}
One time step $=$ build a coupled set of layer equations from the current
temperatures and material properties, then solve them for the new
temperatures. The averaging of ``now'' and ``next'' is what keeps it
stable.
\end{keybox}

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Step 3 --- solving the layer equations fast}

\section{Method: a tridiagonal system}
Because each layer touches only its two immediate neighbours, the linked
equations form a special, easy shape called \textbf{tridiagonal} (only
the diagonal and its two neighbours are non-zero). There is a classic,
very fast recipe for solving these --- the \textbf{Thomas algorithm} ---
that sweeps down once and back up once, instead of the slow general
method. For thousands of layers this is the difference between instant
and sluggish.

\section{Code: the Thomas algorithm}
\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def _thomas(a, b, c, d):           # solve the tridiagonal system A x = d
    n = b.size
    cp = np.empty(n); dp = np.empty(n); x = np.empty(n)
    cp[0] = c[0]/b[0];  dp[0] = d[0]/b[0]
    for i in range(1, n):                      # forward sweep
        m = b[i] - a[i]*cp[i-1]
        cp[i] = c[i]/m if i < n-1 else 0.0
        dp[i] = (d[i] - a[i]*dp[i-1]) / m
    x[n-1] = dp[n-1]
    for i in range(n-2, -1, -1):               # back substitution
        x[i] = dp[i] - cp[i]*x[i+1]
    return x
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
The first loop sweeps from the surface down, eliminating each layer's
dependence on the one above it (\texttt{cp}, \texttt{dp} are the
cleaned-up coefficients). The second loop sweeps back up, reading off the
new temperature \texttt{x} of each layer from the one below it. Output
\texttt{x} is the temperature of every layer after this time step.
\end{codebox}

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Step 4 --- the surface: balancing sunlight and glow}

\section{Physics: the surface energy balance}
The top layer is special: its temperature is whatever makes energy
balance at every instant. Sunlight absorbed must equal infrared light
radiated away \emph{plus} heat conducted downward into the soil:

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\underbrace{(1-A)\,F_\odot}_{\text{sunlight absorbed}}
\;-\;
\underbrace{\varepsilon\,\sigma\,T_s^4}_{\text{infrared glow out}}
\;-\;
\underbrace{K\,\dfrac{T_s - T_\text{below}}{\Delta z/2}}_{\text{conducted downward}}
\;=\; 0
\]
\textbf{$A$} albedo (fraction of sunlight reflected, so $1-A$ is
absorbed); \textbf{$F_\odot$} the incoming sunlight; \textbf{$\varepsilon$}
emissivity; \textbf{$\sigma$} the Stefan--Boltzmann constant; \textbf{$T_s$}
the surface temperature we are solving for. The glow grows as
$T_s^4$ --- very steeply --- which is why this cannot be solved by simple
algebra.
\end{mathbox}

\section{Method: Newton's method finds $T_s$}
Because $T_s$ appears as $T_s^4$, there is no tidy formula for it. So the
code \emph{guesses, checks the imbalance, and corrects} --- repeatedly,
using \textbf{Newton's method}, which uses the slope of the imbalance to
make each correction. It converges in a handful of tries.

\section{Code: the imbalance and the solver}
\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def surface_energy_balance_residual(T_s, insolation, albedo, emissivity,
                                    K_surf, dz_surf, T_subsurf):
    radiative_in  = (1.0 - albedo) * insolation        # sunlight absorbed
    radiative_out = emissivity * SIGMA_SB * T_s**4      # infrared glow
    conductive    = K_surf * (T_s - T_subsurf) / (0.5*dz_surf)  # into soil
    return radiative_in - radiative_out - conductive   # the imbalance
\end{lstlisting}

\codefile{lunar/solver.py (Newton iteration, trimmed)}
\begin{lstlisting}
for _ in range(max_iter):
    R  = surface_energy_balance_residual(T_s, ...)     # current imbalance
    dR = -4.0*emissivity*SIGMA_SB*T_s**3 - 2.0*K_surf/dz_surf  # its slope
    T_s_new = T_s - R/dR                               # Newton correction
    if abs(T_s_new - T_s) < tol:
        return T_s_new                                 # converged
    T_s = T_s_new
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
The first function returns the energy imbalance \texttt{R} for a trial
surface temperature: zero means balanced. Newton's update
\texttt{T\_s\_new = T\_s - R/dR} nudges the guess toward balance using the
imbalance's slope \texttt{dR}; when the nudge becomes tiny the surface
temperature has been found for this instant.
\end{codebox}

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Step 5 --- the bottom: the geothermal flux}

\section{Physics}
At the bottom of the column we impose the steady heat from the interior:
a fixed upward flux $Q_b$. This is what makes the deep temperature climb
with depth in the first place --- the column is fed from below, not
sealed off (sealing it off was a conceptual error the project explicitly
avoids).

\section{Code}
In the time-step assembly, the bottom layer simply receives an extra
dollop of heat each step:

\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
d[-1] += dt * inputs.Q_b / cap[-1]     # steady heat into the bottom layer
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
\texttt{d[-1]} is the bottom layer's known right-hand side; adding
\texttt{dt*Q\_b/cap[-1]} injects the geothermal heat ($Q_b$ for a time
\texttt{dt}, divided by the layer's heat capacity) every step. That one
line is the entire bottom boundary condition.
\end{codebox}

% =====================================================================
\chapter{Step 6 --- settling to equilibrium}\label{ch:equilibrium}

\section{Physics: why settling is slow}
With the recipe above the computer can march the lunar day forward step
by step. But to get the \emph{settled} repeating state, it must run until
the column forgets wherever it started. The surface skin forgets in a few
lunar days; the deep soil, being such a good insulator, takes the
equivalent of \emph{roughly a thousand} lunar days. Marching that long
for every trial $K_d$ would be hopelessly slow.

\section{Method and code: the shortcut}
The fix uses the steady-state fact from Chapter~5: once settled, the same
heat must flow through every layer, which fixes the entire deep profile
directly --- no waiting. The \textbf{equilibrium solver} alternates a
short march (to settle the fast skin) with a direct reconstruction of the
deep part, repeating until nothing changes.

\codefile{lunar/equilibrium.py (the outer loop, trimmed)}
\begin{lstlisting}
for it in range(1, max_outer + 1):
    out = solve_pixel(... n_lunations_spinup=n_inner ...)  # short march
    T_mean = out.T.mean(axis=1)                            # cycle-average
    anchor = T_mean[i0]                                    # temp at 0.55 m
    drift  = abs(anchor - anchor_prev)
    # rebuild the deep profile from steady-state heat balance:
    T_init = _reconstruct_subskin(T_mean, z, i0, Q_b, K_func, u_rect)
    if drift < anchor_tol_K and it >= 2:
        break                                              # converged
    anchor_prev = anchor
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{codebox}
Each pass does a short simulation (\texttt{solve\_pixel}), reads the
settled temperature at an anchor depth (0.55\,m), then rebuilds the deep
profile directly from the heat-balance law
(\texttt{\_reconstruct\_subskin}). When the anchor stops moving between
passes (\texttt{drift < anchor\_tol\_K}), the true settled state has been
reached --- typically after a handful of passes, at the cost of the old
short run rather than a thousand-day march.
\end{codebox}

\begin{keybox}
Solving the model $=$ grid (Ch.\ 6) $+$ time-stepping each layer (Ch.\ 7--8)
$+$ the two boundary rules (Ch.\ 9--10) $+$ settling to equilibrium
(Ch.\ 11). Those five pieces are the entire forward model. Why the
settling step matters so much gets its own treatment in
Chapter~\ref{ch:bugfix}.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{The Apollo data and the retrieval}

\section{The data}
On Apollo 15 (1971) and 17 (1972), astronauts lowered thermometers more
than two metres down (Figure~\ref{fig:probe}); the records were restored
from the original tapes in 2018. Two honest housekeeping steps: discard
every sensor above 80\,cm (the probe stem leaks surface heat down them ---
the ``borestem cut''), and for each remaining sensor average its long,
settled tail into one equilibrium temperature. Result: 7 trustworthy
points at Apollo 15, 16 at Apollo 17 --- 23 numbers in total.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/fig_intro_probe.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The probe.} A fibreglass stem with thermometers at
several depths. Shaded band ($z<80$\,cm): discarded. Right panel: the two
textbook conductivity models compared later.}
\label{fig:probe}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_context_map.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Where the boreholes are.} Apollo 15 on a mare/highland
boundary; Apollo 17 in a lava-filled valley --- different geology, a
physical reason the two sites could differ.}
\label{fig:map}
\end{figure}

\section{The retrieval: guess, simulate, compare}
We cannot measure $K_d$ directly, so we reverse the problem
(Figure~\ref{fig:retrieval}a): guess a $K_d$, simulate the temperatures
it produces, score the mismatch, and keep the best.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_primer_retrieval.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Extracting a number.} (a) The guess--simulate--compare
loop. (b) The real result: mismatch (RMSE) versus guessed $K_d$ forms a
bowl whose bottom is the best fit --- Apollo 15 near 4.6, Apollo 17 near
8.1; dashed line is the single global value (3.4).}
\label{fig:retrieval}
\end{figure}

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\big(T^{\text{model}}_i - T^{\text{data}}_i\big)^2}
\]
For each of $N$ sensors take the model$-$data gap, square it (big misses
count more, sign ignored), average, square-root back to degrees. Zero is
perfect.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{keybox}
The retrieved conductivity is the bottom of the mismatch bowl: 4.6 at
Apollo 15, 8.1 at Apollo 17 --- both above the global 3.4, and different
from each other.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Uncertainty I: the bootstrap}

\section{The question and the trick}
With only 7 or 16 points, how sure are we? We cannot redo Apollo, but we
can \emph{resample} our own data: treat the sensors as a bag of tickets
and draw a new set \emph{with replacement} (some sensors picked twice,
some left out). Each fake set gives a slightly different $K_d$; doing this
1{,}500 times, the spread of answers is the uncertainty
(Figure~\ref{fig:bootstrap}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_bootstrap.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The bootstrap, from scratch.} (a) Your real sensors.
(b) Many resampled sets (repeats allowed, some left out); each yields its
own $K_d$. (c) The spread of those answers is the error bar --- 95\% of
Apollo 15's resamples fall in [4.1, 7.5].}
\label{fig:bootstrap}
\end{figure}

\begin{plainbox}
Shuffling your own sample mimics the spread you would get from repeating
the whole experiment --- letting a tiny dataset honestly report its own
reliability, with no assumptions about bell curves.
\end{plainbox}

\begin{keybox}
Bootstrap $=$ measure uncertainty by reshuffling your own data many times
and watching the answer jump. Small jumps mean confident. The two sites'
difference stays positive in $\sim$99\% of reshuffles --- that is what
``statistically significant'' means here.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Uncertainty II: Bayesian inference and MCMC}

\section{Updating belief with data}
A complementary method lets us fold in prior knowledge of the heat flux
$Q_b$. \textbf{Bayesian inference} combines the \textbf{prior} (what we
believed before), the \textbf{likelihood} (what the data say), into the
\textbf{posterior} (updated belief), sharper than either
(Figure~\ref{fig:mcmc}a).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_mcmc.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Bayesian updating and the ridge.} (a) Prior $\times$
likelihood $=$ posterior. (b) Our 2-D landscape: plausibility is high all
along a diagonal \emph{ridge} because the data fix the \emph{ratio}
$Q_b/K_d$, not each separately. Dots are MCMC ``footprints''; the star is
the published value.}
\label{fig:mcmc}
\end{figure}

\section{Why MCMC, and what the ridge means}
With two unknowns ($K_d$ and $Q_b$) the posterior is a landscape we
cannot write down, so \textbf{Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)} explores
it with random ``walkers'' that wander toward more-plausible spots,
leaving footprints whose density maps the answer. It reveals a long
diagonal \textbf{ridge} (Figure~\ref{fig:mcmc}b): one borehole fixes only
the gradient, i.e.\ the ratio $Q_b/K_d$ --- halve both and nothing
changes. This is why absolute $K_d$ depends on the assumed $Q_b$, while
the \emph{difference} between sites is sturdier.

\begin{keybox}
Bayesian/MCMC $=$ explore every answer consistent with data and prior
knowledge using random walkers. For us it confirms the bootstrap and
exposes the $K_d$--$Q_b$ ridge.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Choosing between explanations: the AICc}

\section{The overfitting trap}
Two stories compete: ``different conductivity per site'' versus ``same
conductivity, different heat flux.'' We cannot just pick whichever fits
closer, because a model with more free knobs can \emph{always} fit
better, even by chasing noise --- \textbf{overfitting}
(Figure~\ref{fig:aicc}a).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_aicc.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Overfitting and the AICc.} (a) Too simple misses the
trend; too complex chases noise; the middle is right. (b) The AICc adds a
penalty for extra knobs and scores our three real models: M1 (per-site
$K_d$) wins.}
\label{fig:aicc}
\end{figure}

\section{A fair scoreboard}
The \textbf{AICc} scores each model as its misfit \emph{plus a penalty
per free parameter}; lowest wins --- good fit without needless
complexity.

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\mathrm{AICc} =
\underbrace{N\ln(\text{misfit}/N)}_{\text{rewards fit}}
+ \underbrace{2k}_{\text{penalty per knob}}
+ \underbrace{\frac{2k(k+1)}{N-k-1}}_{\text{small-sample fix}}
\]
\textbf{$N$} data points, \textbf{$k$} free parameters. Only differences
between models matter: a gap $\gtrsim2$ is meaningful, $\gtrsim10$
decisive.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{keybox}
AICc $=$ a scoreboard rewarding fit but fining complexity. It picks the
per-site-conductivity explanation over one-size-fits-all.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{The mistake we found and fixed}\label{ch:bugfix}

\section{The bug}
To start a simulation the computer needs an initial deep temperature. The
old code typed one in (250\,K for Apollo 15, 255\,K for Apollo 17) and
ran only 30 lunar days --- far short of the $\sim$1000 needed to settle.
So the deep temperature was mostly the typed-in guess, and the
conductivity read from it was partly an echo of that guess. We proved it
(Figure~\ref{fig:anchorfix}a): three starting guesses gave three
different deep profiles. An answer that moves with an arbitrary guess is
not a measurement.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_primer_anchorfix.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The bug and the fix} (real runs, Apollo 15). (a) Old
method: starting from 245/250/255\,K, the deep profile fans out --- the
guess leaks in. (b) New method: all three land on one curve through the
data. The answer no longer depends on the guess.}
\label{fig:anchorfix}
\end{figure}

\section{The fix and the check}
The equilibrium solver of Chapter~\ref{ch:equilibrium} computes the
settled state directly. We verified it: starting from 240 vs 260\,K now
agrees to within $0.02$\,K, and a long check-run does not drift
(Figure~\ref{fig:anchorfix}b). We did \emph{not} just ``run longer'' --
we used the heat-balance shortcut to jump to the answer.

\section{What changed}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Apollo 15: $4.86\to4.58$} --- barely moved (its guess was
      close by luck).
\item \textbf{Apollo 17: $11.23\to8.12$} --- dropped a lot (its guess was
      too warm and had inflated the value).
\end{itemize}

\begin{keybox}
The fix made the science \emph{stronger}: the site difference became
significant, Apollo 17's value moved into the physically expected range,
and an independent model that used to contradict it now agrees. When a
fix makes independent checks line up, it is the right fix.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Honest limits and the bottom line}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Absolute values lean on $Q_b$.} The data fix the ratio
      $Q_b/K_d$ (the ridge), so revising the heat flux shifts $K_d$
      proportionally. The site \emph{difference} is far more robust.
\item \textbf{Only two boreholes exist.} Two points cannot map the whole
      Moon --- only show these two differ. The rest needs new missions.
\item \textbf{Apollo 17 is systematics-sensitive.} Its error bar is
      tight, but its accuracy depends on a few inputs, reported openly in
      the paper's error budget.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Rock-solid:} the two sites need different conductivities, Apollo
17 the more conductive. \textbf{Conditional:} the exact numbers, which
depend on the assumed heat flux and model form. The paper says, clearly,
which is which --- and that honesty is what makes it credible.
\end{keybox}

\chapter{Glossary of every term}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=9.5em,style=nextline,font=\bfseries]
\item[Conduction] Heat spreading through solid material, atom to atom.
\item[Temperature / heat] Average atomic jiggle / the energy of jiggling
  on the move.
\item[Thermal conductivity ($K$, $K_d$)] How easily heat passes; $K_d$ is
  the deep settled value --- our target. Small $=$ good insulator.
\item[Heat flux ($q$, $Q_b$)] Heat crossing one m$^2$ per second; $Q_b$
  is the steady flux from the Moon's interior.
\item[Gradient ($\partial T/\partial z$)] Temperature change per metre of
  depth.
\item[Fourier's law] Flux $=$ conductivity $\times$ gradient.
\item[Heat equation] Temperature changes where more heat flows in than
  out.
\item[Density ($\rho$) / specific heat ($c_p$)] Mass per volume / energy
  to warm 1\,kg by 1\,K.
\item[Albedo ($A$) / emissivity ($\varepsilon$)] Fraction of sunlight
  reflected / efficiency of infrared glow.
\item[Skin depth] How far the day--night swing reaches (a few cm).
\item[Steady state / equilibrium] Settled condition, unchanging day to
  day.
\item[Boundary conditions] Rules at the column ends: surface radiation
  balance; fixed flux $Q_b$ at the bottom.
\item[Grid / discretisation] Splitting the column into layers and time
  into steps so a computer can solve the equation.
\item[Finite difference] Replacing a calculus rate-of-change with a
  difference between neighbours.
\item[Crank--Nicolson] A stable time-stepping scheme averaging ``now''
  and ``next.''
\item[Tridiagonal / Thomas algorithm] The simple shape of the layer
  equations, and the fast recipe that solves them.
\item[Newton's method] Guess-and-correct using slope, used for the
  surface temperature.
\item[Borestem cut] Discarding sensors above 80\,cm (stem contamination).
\item[Retrieval] Guessing $K_d$, simulating, keeping the best match.
\item[RMSE] One number for the typical model--data gap, in degrees.
\item[Bootstrap] Resampling your own data to measure uncertainty.
\item[Bayesian / prior / likelihood / posterior] Updating belief: before
  / data / after.
\item[MCMC] Random walkers mapping all plausible answers.
\item[Degeneracy / ridge] When data fix only a combination ($Q_b/K_d$),
  not each unknown.
\item[Overfitting / AICc] Fitting noise / a score that fines complexity.
\item[HFE, A15/A17] Heat-Flow Experiment; the Apollo 15 and 17 sites.
\end{description}
