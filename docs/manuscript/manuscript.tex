\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We measure how well heat conducts through the deep lunar regolith --- the
parameter $K_d$ --- separately at the Apollo~15 and Apollo~17 heat-flow
boreholes, and find them to differ by about a factor of two
($K_{d,\mathrm{A15}}^{*}=4.58$, $K_{d,\mathrm{A17}}^{*}=8.12$
mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$). This document explains \emph{everything} behind that
sentence, assuming no prior knowledge: what heat conduction is and how we model
it; the two published conductivity models and how to reproduce them; the
numerical solver line by line; the subtle bug we found in reaching steady state
and the faster, self-certifying method that replaced it; how $K_d$ is actually
retrieved from the data; how the uncertainty is quantified; why the basal heat
flux $Q_b$ matters; and why the two sites plausibly differ. Figures accompany
every step. Sections are cross-referenced to the actual Python source files so
you can read this document alongside the code.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{What we are trying to do, and why}\label{ch:intro}

\section{The question}\label{sec:question}
Every airless body loses its internal heat through a blanket of broken rock ---
\emph{regolith}. How fast that heat escapes, and how steeply temperature rises
with depth, is controlled by the regolith's \textbf{thermal conductivity}: a
number, $K$, that says how many watts of heat flow through a square metre for
each degree of temperature change per metre of depth (units
W\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$). Deep down, that number settles to a value we call
$K_d$ (``$d$'' for deep).

The Apollo~15 and~17 missions buried temperature probes in the Moon (the
Heat-Flow Experiment, HFE) and recorded subsurface temperatures from 1971 to
1977. The standard practice has been to assume \emph{one} global $K_d$ for the
whole Moon. \textbf{Our question is simply: what is $K_d$ at each of the two
boreholes, separately --- and do they differ?} (Fig.~\ref{fig:map}.)

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/fig_context_map.pdf}
\caption{The two heat-flow boreholes. Apollo~15 sits at Hadley Rille on the
Imbrium mare/Apennine boundary; Apollo~17 in the Taurus--Littrow highland valley.
Different terrains suggest possibly different regolith properties.}
\label{fig:map}
\end{figure}

\section{Why it matters}
$K_d$ sets the deep temperature profile, which feeds models of subsurface ice
stability (relevant for future water-mining missions), habitat thermal design,
drilling engineering, and the Moon's heat budget. A wrong, one-size-fits-all $K_d$
propagates errors into all of them. Getting it right \emph{per site} is the
contribution of this work.

\section{The data we use}
The Apollo HFE probes recorded temperatures at multiple depths, from about 0.5\,m
down to 1.39\,m (Apollo~15) and 2.34\,m (Apollo~17). We use only the \emph{deep
sensors} --- those below 80\,cm --- because they sit below the daily temperature
swing and respond only to the slow, steady geothermal signal. Their temperatures
are stable year to year, which tells us the regolith at those depths has reached
its \emph{periodic steady state}: the thermal profile repeats identically from one
lunation (lunar day, $\sim$29.5 Earth days) to the next.

At Apollo~15 we have 7 deep sensors; at Apollo~17, 16. These are the data points
our model must match.

\section{The shape of the whole argument}
In one breath: \emph{(i)} heat conduction is described by one equation;
\emph{(ii)} two published formulas give $K$ as a function of temperature and
depth; \emph{(iii)} we put one of them in a computer model of the regolith and
run it until it stops changing (``steady state''); \emph{(iv)} we ask which $K_d$
makes the model's deep temperatures match the Apollo sensors; \emph{(v)} the hard
part was reaching steady state correctly --- the bulk of this document --- and
\emph{(vi)} we then quantify the uncertainty and interpret the result. Each clause
is a chapter.

\chapter{The physics, from the ground up}\label{ch:physics}
% =====================================================================

\section{Heat conduction: what Fourier's law says}\label{sec:fourier}
Heat flows from hot to cold. Fourier's law quantifies this: the heat flux $q$
(watts per square metre, W\,m$^{-2}$) through a material is proportional to the
temperature gradient:
\begin{equation}
q = -K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z},
\label{eq:fourier}
\end{equation}
where $z$ is depth (metres), $T$ is temperature (Kelvin), and $K$ is the
thermal conductivity (W\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$). The minus sign says heat flows
\emph{downhill} in temperature: from the hot surface into the cooler subsurface
during the day, and back up at night.

\begin{plain}
Imagine holding one end of a metal rod over a flame. Heat flows from the hot end
to the cold end. $K$ tells you how fast: copper ($K\approx400$) carries heat
quickly; lunar regolith ($K\approx0.003$) is an extraordinary insulator. The
$\partial T/\partial z$ part says heat flows faster when the temperature changes
sharply over a short distance (steep gradient) and slower when it changes
gradually (gentle gradient).
\end{plain}

\section{The heat equation: conservation of energy in a column}\label{sec:heateq}
Now demand energy conservation in a thin slab of regolith. The rate at which the
slab stores heat equals the difference between what flows in through its top face
and what flows out through its bottom face. This bookkeeping gives the
\textbf{heat equation}:
\begin{equation}
\rho(z)\,c_p(T)\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}
   = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\!\left(K(T,z)\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right),
\label{eq:heat}
\end{equation}
where $\rho$ is the density (kg\,m$^{-3}$), $c_p$ is the specific heat capacity
(J\,kg$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$, how much energy it takes to warm a kilogram by one
Kelvin), and $t$ is time (seconds).

\begin{plain}
The left side says ``this layer is warming (or cooling) at such-and-such a
rate.'' The right side says ``because this much more heat is arriving from above
than leaving below (or vice versa).'' The three material properties --- $\rho$
(how heavy), $c_p$ (how hard to heat), and $K$ (how easily heat passes through)
--- are the knobs. This single equation is the physics of the entire study.
\end{plain}

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_conduction.pdf}
\caption{Heat conduction in a column: flux $q=-K\,\partial_z T$ moves heat down
the temperature gradient; the heat equation is the bookkeeping of what each
layer stores versus what it passes on.}
\label{fig:conduction}
\end{figure}

\section{The lunar forcing: why the surface oscillates wildly}\label{sec:forcing}
At the surface, the Sun drives a huge day--night temperature swing. The lunar day
is about 29.5 Earth days long, so the surface bakes for $\sim$14.75 days and then
sits in darkness for $\sim$14.75 days. Surface temperatures swing from
$\sim$100\,K at night ($-173\,$\textdegree C) to $\sim$390\,K at noon
($+117\,$\textdegree C). This is the ``forcing'' that drives everything.

\section{The skin depth: why only the top matters for the daily cycle}\label{sec:skin}
This ``thermal wave'' travels downward, but it \emph{dies out} exponentially. Its
amplitude falls by a factor of $e\approx2.72$ over the \textbf{skin depth}:
\begin{equation}
\delta=\sqrt{\frac{2\kappa}{\omega}},
\label{eq:skin}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa=K/(\rho c_p)$ is the thermal diffusivity (how fast heat spreads)
and $\omega=2\pi/P$ is the angular frequency of the forcing ($P$ = one lunation).
For lunar regolith, $\delta\approx10$--$20$\,cm. Below a few skin depths ---
roughly 50\,cm --- the regolith barely feels day or night. It responds only to
the \emph{average} heating over the whole lunation.

\begin{keypt}
This split --- a fast, shallow daily wave on top of a slow, deep mean temperature
profile --- is the single most important fact for everything that follows.
The surface oscillates wildly; the deep column sits at a nearly constant temperature
that is set by the \emph{mean} energy balance and the upward geothermal flux. Keep
this picture in mind through every chapter.
\end{keypt}

\section{Boundary conditions: what happens at the top and bottom}\label{sec:bc}
The heat equation (Eq.~\ref{eq:heat}) needs a rule at each end of the column ---
these are called \textbf{boundary conditions}.

\subsection{Top (surface): a radiative energy balance}
The surface has no atmosphere above it, so sunlight absorbed must equal heat
radiated away plus heat conducted downward:
\begin{equation}
(1-A)\,S(t) = \varepsilon\sigma T_s^4 + K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\Big|_{z=0},
\label{eq:surfbc}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item $A$ is the albedo (reflectivity, $\sim$0.13): fraction of sunlight bounced away;
\item $S(t)$ is the incident solar flux (W\,m$^{-2}$), which swings between
  $\sim$1361 at noon and 0 at night;
\item $\varepsilon$ is the emissivity ($\sim$0.95): how efficiently the surface
  radiates heat as infrared;
\item $\sigma=5.6704\times10^{-8}$ W\,m$^{-2}$\,K$^{-4}$ is the Stefan--Boltzmann
  constant;
\item $T_s$ is the surface temperature.
\end{itemize}
The $T_s^4$ term makes this equation \emph{non-linear} --- we cannot solve for
$T_s$ by algebra; it must be found numerically each timestep
(\S\ref{sec:newton}).

\begin{plain}
At noon: lots of sunlight arrives, the surface gets very hot, and it radiates that
energy away as infrared ($T^4$ radiation). A tiny bit of heat also conducts down
into the soil. At night: no sunlight, so the surface cools by radiating its stored
heat, and some heat conducts back up from the still-warm subsurface. The balance
between these processes sets $T_s$ at every moment.
\end{plain}

\subsection{Bottom: the geothermal flux}\label{sec:bottombc}
From far below, the Moon's interior supplies a small, steady heat flux $Q_b$
(``basal flux''). This heat comes from radioactive decay in the Moon's interior.
At Apollo~15, the measured value is $Q_b=21$\,mW\,m$^{-2}$; at Apollo~17,
$Q_b=15$\,mW\,m$^{-2}$. These are tiny compared to the $\sim$1361\,W\,m$^{-2}$
noon sunlight, but they set the \emph{deep} temperature gradient.

At the base of our model column, the boundary condition is:
\begin{equation}
K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\bigg|_{z=z_{\max}} = Q_b.
\label{eq:bottombc}
\end{equation}

\begin{plain}
Think of $Q_b$ as a slow trickle of heat rising from below, always on, always
the same. It is so weak that at the surface you would never notice it. But at
depth --- below the daily noise --- that trickle is all there is, and it
controls how steeply the temperature rises with depth. That is exactly the
signal we use to measure $K_d$.
\end{plain}

\section{Periodic steady state: the key concept}\label{sec:fluxclosure}
After long enough, the column reaches a \textbf{periodic steady state}: every
lunation is identical to the last. No net heat is being stored anywhere; the
column has fully adapted to both the surface forcing and the basal flux.

Two conditions define this state, and the second one is the backbone of the
entire method:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textbf{The surface skin repeats:} the shallow daily oscillation is
periodic --- today's temperature cycle looks exactly like yesterday's.
\item \textbf{The deep flux is uniform:} below the skin, there are no heat
sources, so in steady state the same geothermal flux $Q_b$ must pass upward
through \emph{every} layer. Combined with Fourier's law, this fixes the
gradient of the cycle-mean temperature $\Tm$ at every depth:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;K(\Tm,z)\,\frac{d\Tm}{dz}=Q_b
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\frac{d\Tm}{dz}=\frac{Q_b}{K(\Tm,z)}\;}
\label{eq:closure}
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{keypt}
Equation~\eqref{eq:closure} is the backbone of the whole method.
Read it out loud: ``the slope of the mean temperature profile at any depth equals
the basal flux divided by the conductivity at that depth.'' Once you know the
temperature at \emph{one} depth, this equation fixes the temperature at every
depth below it --- because it tells you the slope everywhere. This is not an
approximation; it is energy conservation applied to a column in steady state.
Chapters~\ref{ch:steady} and~\ref{ch:retrieval} are built entirely on this fact.
\end{keypt}

\begin{plain}
Analogy: a river flowing through a pipe of varying width. In steady state, the same
volume of water passes through every cross-section per second (conservation of
mass). Where the pipe is narrow, the water flows fast; where it is wide, the
water flows slowly. Similarly: where $K$ is small (poor conductor, like the
shallow loose regolith), the temperature gradient must be steep to carry the same
$Q_b$ of heat; where $K$ is large (better conductor, like the compacted deep
regolith), the gradient is gentle.
\end{plain}


% =====================================================================
\chapter{The two conductivity models}\label{ch:models}
% =====================================================================
$K$ is not a single number --- it depends on temperature (hotter regolith
radiates across pores, conducting better) and depth (deeper regolith is more
compacted by overlying weight, conducting better). Two published models capture
this.

\section{Hayne et al.\ (2017): the H-parameter model}\label{sec:hayne}

\subsection{The formula}
The solid ``contact'' conductivity rises from a surface value $K_s$ to a deep
value $K_d$ as the regolith compacts, over an $e$-folding depth $H$:
\begin{align}
K_c(z) &= K_d - (K_d-K_s)\,e^{-z/H}. \label{eq:Kc}
\end{align}
At the surface ($z=0$): $K_c=K_d-(K_d-K_s)\cdot 1=K_s$. At great depth
($z\gg H$): the exponential vanishes, $K_c\to K_d$. The parameter $H$ controls
how fast the transition happens.

A radiative term is added on top --- photons can bounce across pore spaces, and
this effect grows with $T^3$:
\begin{align}
K(T,z) &= K_c(z)\,\bigl[\,1+\chi\,(T/T_{\mathrm{ref}})^3\,\bigr].\label{eq:Khayne}
\end{align}

\subsection{The published parameters}
\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrl@{}}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning & Value & Source \\
\midrule
$K_s$ & surface contact conductivity & 0.74 mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ & Hayne (2017) \\
$K_d$ & deep contact conductivity & 3.4 mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ & Hayne (2017), global \\
$H$ & compaction $e$-folding depth & 6 cm & Hayne (2017) \\
$\chi$ & radiative enhancement & 2.7 & Hayne (2017) \\
$T_{\mathrm{ref}}$ & reference temperature & 350 K & Hayne (2017) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{What the code looks like}
In \code{lunar/properties.py}:
\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
def conductivity_hayne(T, z, Ks=7.4e-4, Kd=3.4e-3, H=0.06, chi=2.7):
    Kc = Kd - (Kd - Ks) * np.exp(-z / H)          # contact part: K_s -> K_d
    return Kc * (1.0 + chi * (T / 350.0) ** 3)    # + radiative T^3 term
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Why the literature $K_d$ is 3.4 --- and why we re-measure it}
Hayne et al.\ fit Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Kc}--\eqref{eq:Khayne} to the
\emph{globally averaged} day--night temperature curves measured from orbit by
the Diviner instrument. A single set of parameters reproduced the global average,
and $K_d=3.4$ mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ is that global deep asymptote. It was
never intended to be site-specific. Different geological settings ---
mare basalt versus highland valley debris --- might have genuinely different deep
conductivities. That is the gap we fill.

\begin{plain}
$K_d$ = 3.4 is a whole-Moon average. Our whole project is asking: is the deep
regolith at Apollo~15 the same as at Apollo~17? Spoiler: no. One is about twice the
other.
\end{plain}

The density follows the same exponential shape:
\begin{equation}
\rho(z)=\rho_d-(\rho_d-\rho_s)\,e^{-z/H},\qquad \rho_s=1100,\ \rho_d=1800\ \text{kg\,m}^{-3}.
\end{equation}

\section{Mart\'inez \& Siegler (2021): a density-based model}
An alternative writes $K$ directly in terms of bulk density $\rho$:
\begin{equation}
K(T,\rho)=(A_1\rho+A_2)\,k_{\mathrm{am}}(T)+(B_1\rho+B_2)\,T^3,
\label{eq:Kmartinez}
\end{equation}
with $k_{\mathrm{am}}(T)$ a fitted amorphous-solid polynomial. Same physics,
different parameterisation: where Hayne's free parameter is $K_d$, Mart\'inez's
is effectively the deep density. We use it as an independent cross-check
(Ch.~\ref{ch:crosschecks}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_models.pdf}
\caption{(a) Hayne contact conductivity $K_c(z)$: it rises from $K_s$ at the surface
to its deep asymptote $K_d$. The published global value is 3.4; our retrievals
move it to 4.58 (A15) and 8.12 (A17). (b) Full $K(T)$ at 1\,m for both models ---
the radiative $T^3$ term roughly doubles $K$ by midday.}
\label{fig:models}
\end{figure}

\chapter{The numerical solver, line by line}\label{ch:solver}
% =====================================================================
We cannot solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:heat} with pencil and paper, because $K$
depends on $T$ and $z$ in complicated ways. So we \textbf{discretise}: chop the
column into cells and time into steps, and advance the temperature numerically.
This chapter walks through the solver in \code{lunar/solver.py} and
\code{lunar/grid.py}, piece by piece.

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_numerical.pdf}
\caption{Discretising the column: cells in depth, steps in time; each time step
solves a small linear system that couples each cell to its neighbours.}
\label{fig:numerical}
\end{figure}

\section{The geometric depth grid}\label{sec:grid}
The daily wave needs millimetre resolution at the top (because the temperature
changes rapidly over a few centimetres), but the deep column can be coarse
(because the temperature there barely changes). So the cells grow geometrically
--- each $\sim$8\% thicker than the one above.

In \code{lunar/grid.py}:
\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
def make_geometric_grid(z_max=5.0, dz0=0.002, growth=0.08):
    faces, dz = [0.0], dz0
    while faces[-1] < z_max:
        faces.append(faces[-1] + dz)
        dz *= (1 + growth)              # each layer ~8% thicker
    # cell centres z_mid, thicknesses dz follow from the faces
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{plain}
The top cell is 2\,mm thick (to resolve the daily wave precisely). By a depth
of 1\,m the cells are $\sim$10\,cm thick. By 5\,m they are $\sim$1\,m thick. This
gives $\sim$69 cells total: fine where it matters, cheap where it does not. The
alternative --- uniform 2\,mm cells all the way to 5\,m --- would need 2500 cells
and be $\sim$35$\times$ slower.
\end{plain}

\section{One time step: Crank--Nicolson}\label{sec:cn}
We step forward in time by one hour (3600\,s) per step. There are $\sim$709 steps
in one lunation. At each step, we need to compute the new temperature profile
$T^{n+1}$ from the old one $T^n$. We use the \textbf{Crank--Nicolson} scheme,
which averages the heat fluxes at the old and new time levels. This produces a
tridiagonal linear system $A\,T^{n+1}=d$:

In \code{lunar/solver.py}, function \code{\_step}:
\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
K   = K_func(T_prev, z_mid)              # conductivity at current T, each cell
Kf  = harmonic_mean(K)                   # conductivity at cell faces
cap = rho * cp_func(T_prev) * dz         # heat capacity of each cell [J/m^2/K]
aL  = dt * Kf[:-1] / (dz_c[:-1] * cap)  # left-face coupling coefficient
aR  = dt * Kf[1:]  / (dz_c[1:]  * cap)  # right-face coupling coefficient
# The tridiagonal matrix A has three bands:
a = -0.5*aL                # sub-diagonal (coupling to cell above)
c = -0.5*aR                # super-diagonal (coupling to cell below)
b = 1 + 0.5*(aL + aR)     # main diagonal (self + both neighbours)
# The right-hand side d:
d = 0.5*aL*Tleft + (1 - 0.5*(aL+aR))*T_prev + 0.5*aR*Tright
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{plain}
What this does, in words: each cell's new temperature is a weighted average of its
old temperature and its neighbours' temperatures. Cells with high conductivity
exchange heat faster (larger coupling coefficients $a_L$, $a_R$). Cells with high
heat capacity ($\rho c_p \Delta z$) respond more slowly. The 0.5 factors are the
Crank--Nicolson averaging between old and new time levels, which makes the scheme
second-order accurate and unconditionally stable (no timestep restriction from the
grid spacing).
\end{plain}

\section{Solving the system: the Thomas algorithm}\label{sec:thomas}
A tridiagonal system (each equation involves at most three unknowns) can be
solved in just two passes --- one forward, one backward --- in $O(N)$ time.
This is the \textbf{Thomas algorithm}:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
# Forward elimination: sweep top to bottom
for i in range(1, n):
    m     = b[i] - a[i]*cp[i-1]
    cp[i] = c[i] / m
    dp[i] = (d[i] - a[i]*dp[i-1]) / m
# Back-substitution: sweep bottom to top
x[n-1] = dp[n-1]
for i in range(n-2, -1, -1):
    x[i] = dp[i] - cp[i]*x[i+1]
\end{lstlisting}

This is already JIT-compiled with Numba in the code (\code{\_thomas} in
\code{lunar/solver.py}), so it runs at near-C speed.

\section{The non-linear surface: Newton's method}\label{sec:newton}
The surface energy balance (Eq.~\ref{eq:surfbc}) has a $T_s^4$ term, so we
cannot solve for $T_s$ by simple algebra. We use Newton's method: guess $T_s$,
compute how far the energy balance is from zero (the ``residual'' $R$), compute
the slope of $R$ vs.\ $T_s$ (the ``Jacobian'' $R'$), and update:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
for _ in range(max_iter):           # usually converges in 2-3 steps
    R  = (1-A)*S - eps*sigma*Ts**4 - Ksurf*(Ts - Tsub)/(0.5*dz0)
    dR = -4*eps*sigma*Ts**3 - 2*Ksurf/dz0    # derivative of R w.r.t. Ts
    Ts = Ts - R/dR                            # Newton update
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{plain}
The residual $R$ says: ``absorbed sunlight minus radiated heat minus conducted heat
--- how far from balance are we?'' If $R>0$, the surface is too cold (not radiating
enough); if $R<0$, it is too hot. Newton's method adjusts $T_s$ in the right
direction, overshooting by just the right amount to converge in 2--3 steps.
\end{plain}

\section{One complete lunation: stepping through the day}\label{sec:oneday}
One lunation = $\sim$709 hourly steps. At each step:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
\item Compute the solar flux $S(t)$ at the current time.
\item Solve the surface boundary condition by Newton's method to get $T_s$.
\item Build the tridiagonal system from the current temperatures.
\item Apply the bottom boundary condition: $K\,dT/dz = Q_b$ at the base.
\item Solve the tridiagonal system with the Thomas algorithm to get the new
      temperature profile.
\item Record the temperatures.
\end{enumerate}

After one full lunation we have the complete time-resolved temperature field
$T(z,t)$ for that cycle.

\section{The spin-up: repeating lunations until steady state}\label{sec:spinup}
To find the steady state, we repeat lunations one after another. After each
lunation, we compare the temperature profile to the previous lunation's:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
for cycle in range(1, n_lunations_spinup + 1):
    T_start = T.copy()
    for k in range(1, n_t):              # march through one lunar day
        T = step(T, ...)                 # the inner time-stepping loop
    if max(abs(T - T_start)) < 0.01 and cycle >= 2:
        break                            # "converged" -- but is it really?
\end{lstlisting}

This brute-force approach works \emph{in principle}: given enough lunations, the
profile converges to the true steady state. But \emph{how many} lunations is the
whole problem of the next chapter.


% =====================================================================
\chapter{Reaching steady state: the old method, the bug, and the fix}\label{ch:steady}
% =====================================================================

This is the most important chapter. It explains the central methodological
contribution of this work: why the original approach to reaching steady state was
flawed, what exactly went wrong, and how the new method fixes it. Read this
chapter carefully; everything in the retrieval (Ch.~\ref{ch:retrieval}) depends
on it.

\section{Why we need steady state at all}\label{sec:whyss}

The Apollo sensors recorded temperatures in regolith that had been sitting
undisturbed for billions of years. The thermal profile at depth is in periodic
steady state: it repeats exactly every lunation. Our model must produce this same
steady state before we can compare it to the data.

$K_d$ is a \emph{steady-state property}: its fingerprint is the settled deep
gradient $d\Tm/dz = Q_b/K_d$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:closure}). A higher $K_d$ means
heat passes more easily, so the gradient is \emph{gentler} (temperature rises
more slowly with depth). A lower $K_d$ means the gradient is \emph{steeper}.
Before the model settles, the deep gradient is still forming and reflects the
arbitrary starting guess, not $K_d$.

\begin{warn}
You cannot read $K_d$ from an unsettled profile. The deep gradient in an
unsettled model is contaminated by the initial condition. It would be like
measuring the insulation of a wall while it is still warming up from one side ---
the temperature slope tells you about the warm-up process, not the insulation.
\end{warn}

\section{The old method: a fixed 30-lunation spin-up}\label{sec:oldmethod}

The original code (before this work) solved the problem by running 30 lunations
of the time-stepping loop (\S\ref{sec:spinup}). Start from a flat temperature
guess (e.g., 250\,K everywhere), step through 30 complete lunar days, and declare
the result ``converged.'' The convergence test checked whether the maximum
temperature change between consecutive lunations was below 0.01\,K.

This seems reasonable: 30 lunations is a month of wall-clock compute. The
convergence test passes. But the result was \textbf{wrong}.

\section{The bug: what actually went wrong (flag F1)}\label{sec:f1}

\textbf{The symptom:} the retrieved $K_d$ changed when we changed the starting
temperature. If we initialised the model at 240\,K everywhere, we got one $K_d$;
at 260\,K, we got a different one. But $K_d$ is a property of the regolith, not
of our guess --- the answer must not depend on where we start.

\textbf{The diagnosis:} the column relaxes to steady state on the \emph{diffusive
timescale}:
\begin{equation}
\tau \sim \frac{z_{\max}^2}{\kappa} \approx \frac{(5\ \text{m})^2}{10^{-8}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}} \approx 2.5\times10^9\ \text{s} \approx \mathbf{1000\ \text{lunations}}.
\end{equation}
After 30 lunations, only $\sim$3\% of the relaxation has occurred. The surface
skin (top $\sim$50\,cm) has settled --- it only takes $\sim$10 lunations because
the skin is thin. But the deep column (below $\sim$50\,cm) still holds the
starting guess, essentially untouched.

\textbf{Why the convergence test lied:} the deep drift is so slow --- order of
$10^{-3}$\,K per lunation --- that the cycle-to-cycle change is already below the
0.01\,K threshold while the profile is far from settled. The test measures only
whether progress has \emph{slowed down}, not whether the profile has actually
\emph{arrived}. It's like measuring the deceleration of a car approaching a
traffic light and concluding it has stopped when it's actually still 100 metres
away.

\textbf{The consequence:} the deep sensors at 0.8--2.4\,m sit exactly in the
unsettled zone. Their modelled temperatures are controlled partly by $K_d$ and
partly by the starting guess. When we try to find the $K_d$ that best matches
the data, we are fitting a profile that still remembers our arbitrary starting
temperature. Different starting temperatures lead to different best-fit $K_d$
values. The starting guess has become a hidden free parameter of the retrieval.

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/spinup_filmstrip.pdf}
\caption{The brute-force spin-up shown as snapshots. Green = current profile,
dashed = true steady state, shaded = daily swing. At 30~lunations, only the top
$\sim$0.5\,m has moved; the deep part still sits at the starting guess. The

\label{fig:spinfilm}
\end{figure}

\begin{plain}
Think of it this way: the surface skin equilibrates like a shallow puddle ---
quickly. The deep column equilibrates like a lake --- slowly. Thirty lunations is
enough time for the puddle but not the lake. The old code checked whether the
puddle had settled, said ``yes,'' and assumed the lake had too. It hadn't.
\end{plain}


\section{The fix: the flux-anchored shortcut}\label{sec:newmethod}

The new method avoids waiting for the deep column to relax. Instead of simulating
1000 lunations of heat diffusion, it uses Eq.~\eqref{eq:closure} to
\emph{calculate} the deep profile directly. This section explains it step by
step.

\subsection{The key insight}

Recall the flux closure equation (Eq.~\ref{eq:closure}):
\[
\frac{d\Tm}{dz} = \frac{Q_b}{K(\Tm, z)}.
\]
In steady state, the deep temperature slope at \emph{any} depth is determined by
$Q_b$ (known) and $K$ at that depth (computable once we know the temperature
there). So if we know the temperature at \emph{one} depth below the skin, we can
compute the temperature at the next depth down, and the next, and so on ---
building the entire deep profile without ever simulating the slow diffusive
relaxation.

The one temperature we need is the ``anchor'' --- the cycle-mean temperature at a
depth just below the daily skin, where the skin has already settled.

\subsection{Step A: settle the skin and read the anchor}

A short spin-up (a handful of lunations) is enough to settle the fast surface
skin. After this short run, we read the cycle-mean temperature $\Tm$ at a chosen
\textbf{anchor depth} $z_0$ (0.55\,m in the production code, which is about 3
skin depths --- well below the daily oscillation zone).

\begin{plain}
The skin only needs $\sim$10 lunations to settle because it's thin. We don't
need the deep column to settle yet --- we only need the temperature at 0.55\,m.
\end{plain}

\subsection{Step B: reconstruct the deep profile}\label{sec:reconstruct}

This is the step that replaces waiting 1000 lunations. Starting from the anchor
temperature at 0.55\,m, we ``walk'' downward through the grid, computing the
temperature at each successive depth using the slope rule.

Here is exactly what happens, cell by cell:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textbf{Start at the anchor:} we know $\Tm(z_0)$ from Step~A.
\item \textbf{Compute the slope:} at this depth, $K$ is known (from the Hayne
  formula, using the current temperature), and $Q_b$ is a fixed input. So the
  slope is:
  \[
    \text{slope} = \frac{d\Tm}{dz} = \frac{Q_b}{K(\Tm, z_0)}.
  \]
  Example: with $Q_b = 21$\,mW\,m$^{-2}$ and $K \approx 9$\,mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$
  at depth, the slope is $\sim$2.3\,K/m --- temperature increases by about 2.3\,K
  for each metre deeper.
\item \textbf{Step to the next cell:} the next cell centre is a distance $\Delta z$
  below. The temperature there is:
  \[
    \Tm(z_0 + \Delta z) = \Tm(z_0) + \text{slope} \times \Delta z.
  \]
\item \textbf{Repeat:} move to the new cell, recompute $K$ at the new temperature
  and depth, get the new slope, step to the next cell. Continue all the way to the
  bottom of the column.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/reconstruct_filmstrip.pdf}
\caption{The deep profile reconstruction shown as snapshots. Starting from the
anchor at 0.55\,m (the dot), the code walks downward, computing
$dT/dz = Q_b/K$ at each depth to determine the next temperature.
By the fourth panel the entire deep profile has been built, matching the true
steady state (dashed).}
\label{fig:reconstruct}
\end{figure}

In code (\code{lunar/equilibrium.py}, function \code{\_reconstruct\_subskin}):
\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
T_new = T_mean.copy()                    # keep the skin as-is from Step A
for i in range(i0, N-1):                 # walk downward from the anchor
    dz = z[i+1] - z[i]                   # thickness of this cell
    q  = Q_b - u_rect[i]                 # u_rect = small correction term
    Ki = K(T_new[i], z[i])               # conductivity at this depth and T
    T_half = T_new[i] + 0.5*q/Ki*dz      # midpoint temperature (for accuracy)
    K_half = K(T_half, 0.5*(z[i]+z[i+1])) # midpoint conductivity
    T_new[i+1] = T_new[i] + q/K_half*dz  # THE STEP: T_next = T_here + slope*dz
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{plain}
This is the part that people find hardest to believe: how can we know the deep
temperature \emph{without simulating} it? The answer is that in steady state,
energy conservation fixes the answer. The same $Q_b$ watts flows through
every layer. At each layer, Fourier's law tells us what temperature gradient is
needed to carry that flux through that layer's conductivity. So we just walk
downward, adding ``slope $\times$ step'' at each cell. The deep profile draws
itself.

It is exactly like calculating the slope of a hillside: if you know the elevation
at one point and the slope at every point (from a map), you can compute the
elevation at any other point by adding up slope~$\times$~distance.
\end{plain}

\subsection{The small correction: rectified flux}
There is one subtlety. Near the skin/deep boundary, the daily temperature
oscillation is small but not zero. The oscillation creates a tiny ``eddy'' heat
flux (called the \textbf{rectified flux}, \code{u\_rect}) that slightly modifies
the mean heat transport. The code subtracts this correction: $q = Q_b - u_{\rm rect}$.
Below about 1\,m, the oscillation is negligible and $u_{\rm rect}\approx0$, so
the slope is simply $Q_b/K$.

\subsection{Iterating Steps A and B}\label{sec:iterate}

Steps A and B are repeated a few times. Why? Because:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item Step A settles the skin assuming the deep column is as we left it.
\item Step B reconstructs the deep column using the anchor from Step A.
\item With the new deep column, Step A produces a slightly different anchor
  (because the boundary between skin and deep has shifted a bit).
\item Step B then gives a slightly different deep profile.
\end{itemize}
But each round the changes are smaller. After 3--5 rounds, the anchor temperature
moves by less than 0.005\,K between iterations --- we declare convergence.

In code (\code{lunar/equilibrium.py}, function \code{solve\_periodic\_equilibrium}):
\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
stages = [
    dict(z0=0.25, n_in=4,  max_it=4,  tol=0.10),   # coarse, fast
    dict(z0=0.55, n_in=12, max_it=20, tol=0.005),   # fine, accurate
]
T_init = np.full(N, T_guess)                        # arbitrary starting guess

for stage in stages:
    anchor = find_cell_nearest(z, stage.z0)
    prev = infinity
    for it in range(stage.max_iters):
        out = solve_pixel(T_init, n_lunations=stage.n_in)  # STEP A: settle skin
        T_mean = out.T.mean(axis=1)                         # cycle-mean profile
        u = rectified_flux(out.T, z, K)                     # eddy correction
        T_recon = reconstruct(T_mean, anchor, Q_b, K, u)    # STEP B: deep profile
        drift = abs(T_mean[anchor] - prev)
        if drift < stage.tol and it >= 2:
            break                                            # converged!
        prev = T_mean[anchor]
        T_init = T_recon                        # feed back into next Step A
\end{lstlisting}

The two-stage schedule (first anchor at 0.25\,m, then at 0.55\,m) is an
optimisation: the shallow anchor converges fast and gets the profile into the
right ballpark; the deeper anchor then refines it to high accuracy.

\section{Proof: they give the same answer}\label{sec:agree}

The decisive test has two parts.

\textbf{Part 1 --- the old method is guess-dependent.}
We ran the old brute-force method (30 lunations) from two starting guesses:
250\,K and 200\,K. The two resulting profiles \emph{disagree} at sensor depths by
$\sim$40\,K (Fig.~\ref{fig:overlay}a). The answer you get depends on where you
start --- a sign that 30 lunations is nowhere near enough.

\textbf{Part 2 --- the new method is guess-independent.}
We ran the new flux-anchored method from three wildly different starting
temperatures: 200\,K, 250\,K, and 300\,K. All three produce the \emph{same}
profile to within 21\,mK at sensor depths (Fig.~\ref{fig:overlay}b,c). Different
starting points, same destination.

Why does the old method need so long? Fig.~\ref{fig:conv} shows brute-force error
at sensor depths falling as a power law with lunation count; at 30 lunations, the
error is still $\sim$13\,K. The deep column's thermal timescale is $\tau \sim
10^{3}$ lunations --- the old method was waiting for heat to diffuse; the new
method imposes the answer directly via $dT/dz = Q_b/K$.

\section{Self-certification: how we know each solve is correct}\label{sec:cert}

Every call to \code{solve\_periodic\_equilibrium} returns two diagnostic numbers:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Anchor drift:} how much the anchor temperature changed between the
  last two iterations. Must be $<0.03$\,K; typically 3\,mK ($0.003$\,K). This
  proves the iteration has converged.
\item \textbf{Flux closure:} the maximum relative deviation of the mean flux from
  $Q_b$ at all depths below the anchor:
  $\max\bigl|\langle q\rangle(z) - Q_b\bigr|/Q_b$. Must be $<2\%$. This proves
  the profile satisfies energy conservation.
\end{enumerate}

If either check fails, the code signals that the solve did not converge. This is
exactly the verification the old fixed spin-up could never provide.

\caption{Guess-independence and flux closure --- the returned proof that a solve
reached steady state. Different starting guesses produce the same answer (left);
the flux closes on $Q_b$ to $<2\%$ at all depths (right).}
\label{fig:cert}
\end{figure}

\chapter{Retrieving \texorpdfstring{$K_d$}{Kd} from the data}\label{ch:retrieval}\label{sec:retrieval}
% =====================================================================

Now we have a solver that produces a correct, certified steady-state profile for
any trial value of $K_d$. The retrieval is conceptually simple: try many $K_d$
values, compare each to the data, and pick the best one.

\section{One forward run: \texttt{run\_with}}
The hub of the pipeline turns one trial $K_d$ into one settled profile. This one
function connects the grid, the conductivity model, the solver, and the
equilibrium driver:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
def run_with(site, kd):
    grid  = make_geometric_grid(**GRID)           # 69-cell geometric grid
    t     = np.arange(0, T_LUNAR, DT_STEP)       # one lunation of hourly steps
    insol = (1 - site.albedo) * S0 * cos(...)     # sunlight forcing
    K     = lambda T, z: conductivity_hayne(T, z, Kd=kd)  # Hayne with this K_d
    eq    = solve_periodic_equilibrium(
                grid, t, insol,
                Q_b=site.Q_b, K_func=K, ...)      # -> certified steady state
    return grid.z_mid, eq.T_mean                  # depths and settled mean profile
\end{lstlisting}

Each call to \code{run\_with} takes about 9 seconds (the new equilibrium solver).
It returns the mean temperature at every depth in the grid.

\section{The sweep and the minimum}\label{sec:sweep}
We call \code{run\_with} for a grid of $K_d$ values (28 values for Apollo~15,
spanning $1$--$15$ mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$; 30 for Apollo~17, spanning $3$--$25$).
For each $K_d$, we measure the misfit between the model's deep temperatures and
the Apollo sensor measurements:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RMSE}(K_d)=\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\bigl[\Tm(z_i;K_d)-T^{\rm obs}_i\bigr]^2},
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over the $N$ deep sensors ($N=7$ for A15, $N=16$ for A17).

The best-fit $K_d^{*}$ is the value that minimises this RMSE. We refine the
minimum with a parabolic fit through the three points around the grid minimum:

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py]
rmse    = np.sqrt((residuals**2).mean(axis=0))  # RMSE at each trial K_d
k       = np.argmin(rmse)                        # nearest grid minimum
kd_star = parabola_vertex(kd_grid[k-1:k+2],     # sub-grid refinement
                          rmse[k-1:k+2])
\end{lstlisting}

Each site shows a clean single minimum (Fig.~\ref{fig:sweep}). The curves are
smooth because the solver is deterministic and the steady state is unique for
each $K_d$.

\caption{Deep-sensor RMSE vs.\ trial $K_d$. The minimum is $K_d^{*}$; Apollo~17's
sits at a clearly higher conductivity than Apollo~15's.}
\label{fig:sweep}
\end{figure}

\[
K_{d,\mathrm{A15}}^{*}=4.58,\qquad K_{d,\mathrm{A17}}^{*}=8.12\quad
\mathrm{mW\,m^{-1}\,K^{-1}}.
\]
Apollo~17's deep regolith conducts heat about twice as well as Apollo~15's. Both
are higher than the global literature value of 3.4.

With these retrieved values, the modelled deep temperatures track the data far
better than the one-size-fits-all value (Fig.~\ref{fig:profiles}); at Apollo~17

% =====================================================================
\chapter{How sure are we? (uncertainty)}\label{ch:stats}
% =====================================================================

A point estimate ($K_d^*=4.58$) is not useful without knowing how uncertain it
is. We quantify uncertainty three ways.

\section{The bootstrap}\label{sec:bootstrap}
The bootstrap is a resampling technique that does not assume any particular noise
model. The procedure:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Resample the sensors:} draw $N$ sensors from the original $N$
  \emph{with replacement} (some sensors appear twice, others not at all). Also
  jitter each sensor's depth by a random amount drawn from
  $\mathcal{N}(0,\,2.5\,\text{cm})$ to account for the uncertainty in exactly
  where each sensor is positioned (Nagihara 2018).
\item \textbf{Re-fit $K_d^*$:} run the full sweep on this resampled sensor set.
\item \textbf{Repeat $N_{\rm boot}=1500$ times.}
\item \textbf{Read the spread:} the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the 1500
  $K_d^*$ values give the 95\% confidence interval.
\end{enumerate}

\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/fig_bootstrap.pdf}
\caption{Bootstrap distributions of $K_d^{*}$ (1500 resamples). The two site
distributions barely overlap --- the contrast is statistically real

\end{figure}

Apollo 17 & 8.12 & [6.85, 9.04] & mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ \\
\midrule
Contrast (A17 $-$ A15) & 3.31 & [0.37, 4.58] & $p\approx0.011$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The $p$-value of $\sim$0.011 means: the probability of seeing this large a
contrast (or larger) by chance, if the two sites actually had the same $K_d$, is
about 1\%. The two humps in Fig.~\ref{fig:boot} barely overlap.

\caption{What the bootstrap does, schematically: resample the data many times,

\section{Bayesian cross-check (MCMC)}\label{sec:mcmc}
An independent check: instead of sweeping $K_d$ on a grid, we run a Markov-chain
Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler that explores the full joint posterior in $(K_d, Q_b)$
--- allowing both parameters to vary, not just $K_d$. Even when $Q_b$ is
uncertain, the posterior shows Apollo~17 is more conductive than Apollo~15 about
83\% of the time.

\section{Model selection (AICc)}\label{sec:aicc}
We compare three models:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item $M_1$: different $K_d$ per site, $Q_b$ fixed (our model, 2 free parameters);
\item $M_2$: one shared $K_d$, $Q_b$ fixed (1 free parameter);
\item $M_3$: one shared $K_d$, different $Q_b$ per site (3 free parameters).
\end{itemize}
AICc (Akaike Information Criterion, corrected for small samples) penalises models
for using more parameters. $M_1$ wins: $\Delta\text{AICc}(M_2-M_1) = 3.99$,
$\Delta\text{AICc}(M_3-M_1) = 2.71$. The data favour a \emph{conductivity}
contrast, not a flux artefact.

% =====================================================================
\chapter{The role of the basal flux \texorpdfstring{$Q_b$}{Qb}}\label{ch:qb}\label{sec:qb}
% =====================================================================

$K_d$ cannot be read in isolation, and understanding \emph{why} is essential.

\section{The degeneracy: $K_d$ and $Q_b$ trade off}
The deep gradient is $Q_b/K$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:closure}). For the \emph{same} set of
deep temperatures, you could explain the observed gradient with:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item high $Q_b$ + high $K_d$ (strong flux, easily conducted), or
\item low $Q_b$ + low $K_d$ (weak flux, poorly conducted).
\end{itemize}
Many combinations along a diagonal in $(K_d, Q_b)$ space fit the data almost
equally well. This is the \textbf{$K_d$--$Q_b$ degeneracy}. We cannot separate
the two from a single borehole's temperature measurements alone.

\section{What we do about it}
We \emph{fix} $Q_b$ at the published values ($21$ mW\,m$^{-2}$ for A15, $15$ for
A17) and report $K_d$ \emph{conditional} on them. This is honest: we are not
claiming to have measured $K_d$ independently of $Q_b$. The headline number
$K_d^*=4.58$ means ``the $K_d$ that best fits the data \emph{given} $Q_b=21$.''

\section{The robust claim}
Even though the absolute $K_d$ values shift when $Q_b$ changes, the
\emph{direction} of the contrast survives: Apollo~17 remains more conductive than
Apollo~15 for all plausible $Q_b$ values ($\sim$83\% posterior probability from
the MCMC). $Q_b$ is one of the largest terms in the error budget --- a direct
fingerprint of this coupling.

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/fig_kd_qb_posterior.pdf}
\caption{The $K_d$--$Q_b$ trade-off: many combinations along a diagonal fit
almost equally well, so $K_d$ is conditional on $Q_b$. The contrast between sites
is more robust than either absolute value.}
\label{fig:qb}
\end{figure}

\begin{plain}
If someone challenges your $K_d$ by saying ``but what if $Q_b$ is different?'' ---
acknowledge it: ``yes, $K_d$ rides on $Q_b$. If Apollo~15's $Q_b$ were higher,
our $K_d$ would be higher too. The robust finding is not the absolute number but
the direction: Apollo~17 is more conductive than Apollo~15. That holds regardless
of reasonable $Q_b$ assumptions.''
\end{plain}

\chapter{Why might the two sites differ?}\label{ch:sites}\label{sec:sites}
% =====================================================================
A factor-of-two contrast is large but physically reasonable: the two boreholes
sample genuinely different regolith.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Apollo 15} & \textbf{Apollo 17} \\
\midrule
Setting & Hadley Rille (mare/front) & Taurus--Littrow valley \\
Bond albedo & 0.131 & 0.137 \\
Deepest sensor & 1.39\,m & 2.34\,m \\
Basal flux $Q_b$ (fixed) & 21\,mW\,m$^{-2}$ & 15\,mW\,m$^{-2}$ \\
\textbf{Retrieved $K_d^{*}$} & \textbf{4.58} & \textbf{8.12} mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Heat moves through dry regolith mainly by grain-to-grain contact, so a higher
$K_d$ points to \emph{denser, more compacted, or more rock-rich} deep regolith.
Three plausible contributors at Apollo~17:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Material:} highland massif debris plus dense pyroclastic dark-mantle
  deposits, versus Hadley's mare-basalt regolith.
\item \textbf{Independent support:} the density-based Mart\'inez model prefers a
  higher effective deep density at Apollo~17 (Ch.~\ref{ch:crosschecks}).
\item \textbf{Compaction:} more buried rock fragments or a more mature, sintered
  column at Taurus--Littrow.
\end{enumerate}

The honest framing: \emph{Apollo~17's deep regolith conducts heat about twice as
efficiently as Apollo~15's, conditional on the published basal fluxes.}

\section{Diviner surface closure}

\section{Architecture}
The code is layered (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{\code{lunar/}} --- the physics package. Contains the models
  (\code{properties.py}), the depth grid (\code{grid.py}), the time-stepping
  solver (\code{solver.py}), and the equilibrium driver (\code{equilibrium.py}).
  All constants live in \code{constants.py}; all run configuration in
  \code{config.py}.
\item \textbf{\code{scripts/}} --- thin drivers that call \code{run\_with} and
  produce figures or output files.
\item \textbf{\code{notebooks/}} --- interactive exploration (the equivalence
  demo, the retrieval, the figures).
\item \textbf{\code{tests/}} --- 37 tests including equilibrium certification.
\end{itemize}

One function, \code{run\_with} (in \code{scripts/pipeline/run\_retrieval.py}), is
the hub: it wires together the grid, the conductivity model, and the solver, and
returns a settled profile. Everything else --- the sweep, the bootstrap, the
cross-checks --- calls \code{run\_with} in a loop.

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/fig_architecture.pdf}
\caption{Three layers; imports point only downward. The numerics live in three
small modules (\code{grid}, \code{solver}, \code{equilibrium}).}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

\section{Parallelism: one solve vs.\ many solves}
A single forward solve (one call to \code{run\_with}) is inherently sequential:
each time step depends on the previous one. You cannot split a single trajectory
across cores.

But the \emph{sweep} (28--30 independent $K_d$ values) and the \emph{bootstrap}
(1500 independent resamples) consist of many independent solves. These run
across all available cores. ``More cores speed up the batch, not one solve.''

\section{C++ port: why and what}
The inner time step (\code{\_step} in \code{solver.py}) runs $\sim$709 times per
lunation and $\sim$150 lunations per equilibrium solve --- over 100,000 calls.
The Thomas algorithm is already JIT-compiled (Numba), but the matrix assembly is
interpreted Python --- exactly where a compiled language wins
($\sim$10--100$\times$).

The plan: port \code{grid}, \code{solver}, and \code{equilibrium} to C++; keep
configuration and analysis in Python. Validate the C++ port against the existing
37-test suite and the committed steady-state profiles.

The three speed-ups multiply:
\[
\underbrace{\text{algorithm (shortcut)}}_{\sim30\times,\ \text{done}}
\times
\underbrace{\text{compilation (C++)}}_{\sim10\text{--}100\times}
\times
\underbrace{\text{parallelism}}_{\times\text{cores}}.
\]
Not needed for this paper's $\sim$60 solves; valuable at scale (full Diviner
maps, mission pipeline).

\chapter{Conclusion: how to defend every claim}\label{ch:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{What we did.} Retrieved the deep regolith conductivity separately at
Apollo~15 and~17: $4.58$ and $8.12$ mW\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$.
\item \textbf{How.} A 1-D heat model (Ch.~\ref{ch:physics}--\ref{ch:solver}) with
the Hayne conductivity (Ch.~\ref{ch:models}), solved to a certified steady state
(Ch.~\ref{ch:steady}), with $K_d$ chosen to minimise the deep-sensor misfit
(Ch.~\ref{ch:retrieval}).
\item \textbf{Why the solver is correct.} It is unique by construction, certifies
its own convergence, is guess-independent, and is confirmed by brute force
converging onto it (\S\ref{sec:agree}).
\item \textbf{Why the result is real.} The contrast is statistically significant
(bootstrap $p\approx0.011$, Ch.~\ref{ch:stats}), survives $Q_b$ uncertainty
(83\% posterior, Ch.~\ref{ch:qb}), and is echoed by an independent model
(Ch.~\ref{ch:crosschecks}).
\item \textbf{The honest caveat.} $K_d$ is conditional on the assumed $Q_b$
(Ch.~\ref{ch:qb}); the robust statement is the inter-site \emph{direction}.
\item \textbf{The physics.} Apollo~17's deep regolith conducts heat about twice as
efficiently --- plausibly denser, more compacted, or more rock-rich material
(Ch.~\ref{ch:sites}).
\end{itemize}
