\section{Why anyone studies heat in the Moon}
The Moon looks dead and unchanging, but just beneath its surface there is
a slow, quiet flow of heat that carries information we can get no other
way. Its surface is roasted by the Sun through the two-week lunar day and
freezes through the two-week night, swinging by roughly $250\,^\circ$C.
Yet a single metre down, none of that drama is felt: there the
temperature is set by only two things --- how well the soil conducts
heat, and a faint steady warmth seeping up from the Moon's interior.

The key property of the soil is its \textbf{thermal conductivity} --- how
easily heat passes through it. We write it $K$, and its value deep down
(where the soil is compacted and settled) is $K_d$. Lunar soil is an
extraordinary insulator --- several times better than the styrofoam in a
coffee cup --- which is exactly why surface heat cannot reach far below.

Why it matters: every lunar thermal model today uses \emph{one single
value of $K_d$ for the whole Moon}, never checked against direct
underground data --- because such data exist at only two places in
history, the Apollo 15 and 17 landing sites, where astronauts buried
thermometers in 1971--72.

\begin{keybox}
We use the only two underground temperature records ever taken on the
Moon to test a hidden, universal assumption: is one conductivity value
good enough for the entire Moon? We will build, step by step, to the
answer --- no: the two sites differ by about a factor of two, and the
difference is real.
\end{keybox}

\section{Heat, temperature, and the law of conduction}
\subsection{What heat and temperature actually are}
Everything is made of atoms, and atoms are always jiggling.
\textbf{Temperature} measures the \emph{average} jiggle: hot means
vigorous jiggling, cold means sluggish. \textbf{Heat} is the
\emph{energy} of that jiggling on the move --- energy flowing from a
more-jiggly place to a less-jiggly one. Temperature is measured in kelvin
(K): $0\,$K is no jiggling at all (about $-273\,^\circ$C), water freezes
at $273\,$K, and lunar deep soil sits near $250\,$K ($-23\,^\circ$C).

\subsection{Conduction}
When a hot region touches a cold region, the fast atoms jostle their
slower neighbours, passing energy along (Figure~\ref{fig:conduction}). No
material moves --- only energy does, handed atom to atom. It is how a
metal spoon's handle warms in hot soup, and it is the \emph{only} way
heat travels inside airless lunar soil (no air to carry it, and the gaps
are too small for much convection).

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_conduction.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Conduction.} Heat always flows from hot to cold. How
fast depends on the conductivity $K$ and on how steep the temperature
change is --- a steeper change, or a better conductor, moves more heat
per second.}
\label{fig:conduction}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Two words we need: flux and gradient}
\textbf{Heat flux} ($q$) is how much heat energy crosses one square metre
each second, in watts per square metre (W\,m$^{-2}$). \textbf{Temperature
gradient} is how fast temperature changes with distance --- here, with
depth $z$. If the temperature rises 2 degrees over half a metre, the
gradient is $4\,$K per metre. We write it $\partial T/\partial z$, which
is just shorthand for ``the slope of the temperature-versus-depth line.''

\subsection{Fourier's law: the rate of heat flow}
Two centuries ago Joseph Fourier wrote the rule connecting these: heat
flux equals conductivity times gradient.

\begin{mathbox}
\[
q \;=\; -\,K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}
\]
\textbf{$q$} --- heat flux (W\,m$^{-2}$). \textbf{$K$} --- conductivity
(W\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$); a big $K$ passes heat easily. \textbf{$\partial
T/\partial z$} --- the temperature gradient (K\,m$^{-1}$). The minus sign
means heat flows \emph{down} the gradient, from hot toward cold.
\end{mathbox}

\emph{A worked number.} Deep lunar soil conducts about
$K\approx0.009\,$W\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ and its temperature rises about
$1.8\,$K per metre at Apollo 17. So the upward heat flux is
$q = 0.009\times1.8 \approx 0.016\,$W\,m$^{-2}$ --- about a hundred-thousandth
of the noon sunlight ($\sim$1360\,W\,m$^{-2}$). That tiny trickle is the
basal heat flux we will keep meeting.

\begin{plainbox}
A crowded room empties through a door faster if the door is wide (big
$K$) and if it is far more crowded inside than out (steep gradient). Heat
is the crowd; Fourier's law is the turnstile count.
\end{plainbox}

\section{The materials: turning physics into numbers and code}
Fourier's law needs the conductivity $K$; once things change in time we
also need density $\rho$ and specific heat $c_p$. This is our first
``physics, then code'' pairing.

\subsection{Conductivity that grows with depth and temperature}
Two physical facts shape lunar conductivity. \textbf{Depth:} surface dust
is fluffy and loosely packed (a poor conductor); deeper down it is
compressed, so grains touch better and conduct better --- $K$ rises from a
small surface value $K_s$ to a larger deep value $K_d$ over a short
transition depth $H$. \textbf{Temperature:} heat also crosses the tiny
gaps between grains as infrared light (radiation), which grows fast with
temperature (as $T^3$), so warm soil conducts a bit better --- the
``radiative term,'' controlled by a coefficient $\chi$.

\begin{mathbox}
\[
K(T,z) = \underbrace{\big[\,K_d - (K_d-K_s)\,e^{-z/H}\,\big]}_{\text{grows with depth}}
\;\times\;
\underbrace{\big[\,1 + \chi\,(T/350)^3\,\big]}_{\text{grows with temperature}}
\]
First bracket: starts at $K_s$ at the surface ($z=0$, where $e^{0}=1$ so
the two $K_d$ terms leave $K_s$) and climbs toward $K_d$ deep down (where
$e^{-z/H}\to0$). Second bracket: 1 plus the radiative boost; $\chi$ sets
its strength, 350\,K is a reference temperature. $K_d$ is the single
number we retrieve.
\end{mathbox}

\emph{Worked numbers, with the real constants}
($K_s=7.4\times10^{-4}$, $K_d=3.4\times10^{-3}$\,W\,m$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$,
$H=0.06\,$m, $\chi=2.7$): at the surface the depth bracket is just $K_s$;
by $z=0.2\,$m, $e^{-0.2/0.06}=e^{-3.3}\approx0.036$, so it has reached
$\sim$97\% of $K_d$. The radiative bracket at $250\,$K is
$1+2.7\times(250/350)^3 = 1+2.7\times0.36 \approx 2.0$ --- it
\emph{doubles} the conductivity, which is why forgetting this term is a
known, serious bug.

\begin{plainbox}
Conductivity is not one number but a recipe: ``start small at the dusty
top, rise to $K_d$ once packed, and add a little extra when warm.'' Our
job is to find the one ingredient $K_d$ that makes this recipe match the
buried thermometers.
\end{plainbox}

Here is that formula in code --- each line matches a bracket above.
\codefile{lunar/properties.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def conductivity_hayne(T, z, Ks=K_SURFACE, Kd=K_DEEP,
                       H=H_PARAMETER, chi=CHI_RADIATIVE):
    T = np.asarray(T, dtype=np.float64)
    z = np.asarray(z, dtype=np.float64)
    Kc = Kd - (Kd - Ks) * np.exp(-z / H)           # grows with depth
    return Kc * (1.0 + chi * (T / T_REFERENCE)**3)  # times radiative boost
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
\texttt{Kc = Kd - (Kd - Ks)*np.exp(-z/H)} is the first bracket exactly
(\texttt{np.exp(-z/H)} is $e^{-z/H}$). The return multiplies by the
radiative bracket \texttt{(1.0 + chi*(T/T\_REFERENCE)**3)}, where
\texttt{**3} is ``cubed'' and \texttt{T\_REFERENCE}$=350$. Wrapping
\texttt{T} and \texttt{z} in \texttt{np.asarray} lets NumPy compute every
depth at once, in one shot, instead of looping.
\end{codebox}

\subsection{Density and specific heat: how much heat the soil stores}
When temperatures change in time we need how much energy it takes to warm
the soil: its \textbf{density} $\rho$ (mass per cubic metre) and
\textbf{specific heat} $c_p$ (energy to warm one kilogram by one degree).
Density rises with depth as the soil compacts, with the same exponential
shape as the conductivity:
\begin{mathbox}
\[
\rho(z) = \rho_d - (\rho_d - \rho_s)\,e^{-z/H}
\]
Surface density $\rho_s\approx1100$ climbing to deep density
$\rho_d\approx1800\,$kg\,m$^{-3}$ over the same depth scale $H$.
\end{mathbox}
\codefile{lunar/properties.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def density_hayne(z, rho_s=RHO_SURFACE, rho_d=RHO_DEEP, H=H_PARAMETER):
    z = np.asarray(z, dtype=np.float64)
    return rho_d - (rho_d - rho_s) * np.exp(-z / H)
\end{lstlisting}

Specific heat depends on temperature (cold soil stores heat differently
from warm soil) and is a polynomial fit to laboratory measurements of
Apollo samples:
\codefile{lunar/properties.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def _cp_hayne(T):                  # c_p(T): a 4th-order polynomial
    T = np.asarray(T, dtype=np.float64)
    return (CP_HAYNE_C0 + CP_HAYNE_C1*T + CP_HAYNE_C2*T**2
            + CP_HAYNE_C3*T**3 + CP_HAYNE_C4*T**4)
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
Just $c_0 + c_1 T + c_2 T^2 + c_3 T^3 + c_4 T^4$ --- five measured
constants times powers of temperature, added up. Near $250\,$K it gives
$c_p\approx670\,$J\,kg$^{-1}$\,K$^{-1}$ (about a sixth of water's): it
takes 670 joules to warm one kilogram of regolith by one degree.
\end{codebox}

\subsection{Where the numbers come from}
A rule of this project: \emph{no number is invented}. Every constant
lives in one file with its literature source attached.
\codefile{lunar/constants.py}
\begin{lstlisting}

#: Deep contact conductivity [W m^-1 K^-1] -- Hayne et al. (2017).

\begin{keybox}
The physics enters the computer as three small functions ---
\texttt{conductivity\_hayne}, \texttt{density\_hayne},
\texttt{specific\_heat} --- each a direct transcription of a formula, fed
by cited constants. Understand the three formulas and you understand
every material input to the model.
\end{keybox}

\section{The heat equation: bookkeeping for energy}
Fourier's law gives the flow through one surface. To follow temperature
\emph{everywhere, over time}, we add one idea: \textbf{energy
bookkeeping}. Take a thin slice of soil. In one moment, some heat flows
in through its top face and some flows out the bottom face (each given by
Fourier's law). If more comes in than leaves, the leftover energy stays
and warms the slice; if more leaves, it cools. Write that balance down,
and you get the \textbf{heat equation} --- the master equation the whole
program solves.

\begin{mathbox}
\[
\underbrace{\rho\,c_p\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}}
  _{\text{rate heat is stored in the slice}}
\;=\;
\underbrace{\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\!\left(K\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right)}
  _{\text{(heat flowing in)} - \text{(heat flowing out)}}
\]
\textbf{Left:} how fast the slice warms ($\partial T/\partial t$, degrees
per second) times how much heat that costs ($\rho c_p$, the slice's
``thermal inertia''). \textbf{Right:} the net conduction --- Fourier's
flux $K\,\partial T/\partial z$ differenced across the slice (the outer
$\partial/\partial z$ asks ``how much more flux leaves than enters''). If
the right side is zero, in equals out and the slice holds steady.
\end{mathbox}

\begin{plainbox}
``Temperature here changes because more heat arrives than leaves.'' That
one sentence \emph{is} the heat equation. Everything the computer does is
apply it to thousands of thin slices, over and over, as the lunar day
passes.
\end{plainbox}

This equation cannot be solved with pencil and paper for a real,
property-varying column, so we solve it numerically --- the whole of
Part~II.

\section{The lunar column: forcing, skin, and the steady zone}
\subsection{Heated from both ends}
Our column of soil (Figure~\ref{fig:heatflow}a) is driven from
\emph{above} by sunlight (and night-time cooling) and from \emph{below}
by a steady warmth from the interior --- the \textbf{basal heat flux}
$Q_b$, a few thousandths of a watt per square metre (21 at Apollo 15, 15
at Apollo 17, in milliwatts per square metre).

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/fig_primer_heatflow.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The lunar soil column.} (a) Sunlight heats from above;
$Q_b$ rises from below. Top zone (coral): the day--night swing. Deep zone
(teal): steady. Squares: the buried thermometers. (b) The daily swing
collapses with depth --- below the noise by 80\,cm, which is why deep
sensors read a steady value.}
\label{fig:heatflow}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The skin depth: why the daily swing dies out}
The day--night wave is absorbed and smeared by each layer it passes, so
it shrinks fast with depth (Figure~\ref{fig:heatflow}b). The depth over
which it fades is the \textbf{skin depth}, $\delta=\sqrt{2\kappa/\omega}$,
where $\kappa=K/(\rho c_p)$ is the ``thermal diffusivity'' and $\omega$
is the day--night rhythm. For lunar soil $\delta$ is only a few
centimetres; the swing shrinks by a factor $e\approx2.7$ every skin
depth, so by 80\,cm (more than ten skin depths) it has shrunk by
$e^{-10}\approx1/22000$ --- far below what any thermometer can read.

\begin{plainbox}
Press a warm hand on a thick quilt: the surface warms, a finger-width in,
nothing. The Moon's daily heating is that hand --- it reaches only the
top few centimetres. Everything deeper feels only the steady average.
\end{plainbox}

\subsection{The steady deep zone and the gradient law}
Below the skin depth the soil is in \textbf{steady state}: its
temperature no longer wobbles, only climbs slowly with depth as $Q_b$
trickles up. There the time term of the heat equation vanishes and it
collapses to the simple law that lets us measure $K_d$:
\begin{mathbox}
\[
\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta z} = \frac{Q_b}{K_d}
\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
K_d = \frac{Q_b}{\Delta T/\Delta z}
\]
The deep temperature climbs at a steady rate equal to the flux $Q_b$
divided by the conductivity $K_d$. We \emph{measure} the gradient from
the buried thermometers and \emph{know} $Q_b$, so we solve for $K_d$.
\end{mathbox}
\begin{keybox}
This is the whole study in one line: read the deep temperature gradient,
divide the known heat flux by it, get the conductivity. Everything else
is doing this carefully --- with the full time-dependent model instead of
the shortcut, and with honest error bars.
\end{keybox}

\section{Step 1 --- the depth grid}
\subsection{Physics: put the detail where the action is}
All the fast change happens near the surface (the skin). So instead of
slicing the column into equal layers, we make the top layers extremely
thin (2\,mm) and let each successive layer be a little thicker. This
spends computing effort where temperature changes fastest and saves it
deep down where nothing moves. (A uniform grid is forbidden in this
project for exactly this reason --- it would either miss the skin or
waste effort.) The production column is 5\,m deep and ends up with a few
hundred layers.

\subsection{Code: building the grid}
\codefile{lunar/grid.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def make_geometric_grid(z_max=3.0, dz0=0.002, growth=0.15):
    faces = [0.0]
    dz = dz0
    while faces[-1] < z_max:
        faces.append(faces[-1] + dz)   # next layer boundary
        dz *= 1.0 + growth             # each layer thicker by `growth`
    z_face = np.asarray(faces)
    dz_arr = np.diff(z_face)                   # layer thicknesses
    z_mid  = 0.5 * (z_face[:-1] + z_face[1:])  # layer centres
    return DepthGrid(z_face=z_face, z_mid=z_mid, dz=dz_arr)
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
Start at the surface with a 2\,mm layer (\texttt{dz0=0.002}). Each loop
adds the next boundary, then grows the thickness by a few percent
(\texttt{dz *= 1+growth}), so layers gently fatten until the column
reaches \texttt{z\_max}. \texttt{z\_face} are the boundaries,
\texttt{z\_mid} the centres (where temperature lives), \texttt{dz} the
thicknesses. That is the entire scaffolding the solver sits on.
\end{codebox}

\section{Step 2 --- from calculus to arithmetic}
\subsection{Method: differences instead of derivatives}
The heat equation is written in calculus (the $\partial$ symbols mean
``rate of change''). A computer cannot do calculus, but it can do
arithmetic on our layers. The trick (the \textbf{finite-difference} or
\textbf{control-volume} method, Figure~\ref{fig:numerical}) replaces each
rate of change with a simple difference between neighbours: the gradient
between two layers $\approx$ (their temperature difference) $\div$
(distance between their centres); the heat stored in a layer $\approx$
$\rho c_p \times$ (thickness) $\times$ (temperature change).

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_numerical.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{From smooth to computable.} (a) Real temperature varies
smoothly with depth. (b) The computer represents it as a stack of thin
layers, each one number, updated step by step; fine enough layers
reproduce the smooth curve (dashed).}
\label{fig:numerical}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Conductivity at the boundary between two layers}
Heat passing from layer $i$ to $i{+}1$ crosses the boundary between them,
where the two layers have different conductivities. The physically
correct combination is the \textbf{harmonic mean} --- it correctly lets
the worse insulator dominate, exactly like two resistors in series (the
bigger resistor sets the flow):
\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def _face_harmonic_mean(K):           # K = conductivity at each centre
    K_face = np.empty(K.size + 1)
    for i in range(1, K.size):
        K_face[i] = 2.0 * K[i-1]*K[i] / (K[i-1] + K[i])   # harmonic mean
    return K_face
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Stepping forward in time: Crank--Nicolson}
To advance temperature by one time step we use the
\textbf{Crank--Nicolson} scheme: estimate the heat flows using the
\emph{average} of the situation now and one step ahead. Using only
``now'' (fully explicit) can blow up unless the steps are tiny; using
only ``next'' (fully implicit) is stable but less accurate; averaging
both is stable \emph{and} accurate. This turns one time step into a set
of linked equations --- one per layer --- where each layer's new
temperature depends on its own and its two neighbours' new temperatures.

\subsection{Code: assembling one time step}
The solver builds those linked equations as four lists
(\texttt{a},\texttt{b},\texttt{c},\texttt{d}): \texttt{a} and \texttt{c}
are each layer's coupling to the layers above and below, \texttt{b} is
the layer itself, and \texttt{d} is the known right-hand side from the
current temperatures.
\codefile{lunar/solver.py (the heart of \_step, lightly trimmed)}
\begin{lstlisting}
K   = K_func(T_prev, grid.z_mid)        # conductivity at every layer, now
rho = rho_func(grid.z_mid)              # density at every layer
cp  = cp_func(T_prev)                   # specific heat at every layer
cap = rho * cp * dz                     # heat capacity of each layer
K_face = _face_harmonic_mean(K)         # conductivity at the boundaries

# coupling of each layer to its neighbours (how easily heat crosses):
alpha_l[i] = dt * K_face[i]   / (dz_c[i]   * cap[i])   # to layer above
alpha_r[i] = dt * K_face[i+1] / (dz_c[i+1] * cap[i])   # to layer below

# the linked equation for each layer (Crank-Nicolson, theta = 0.5):
a[i] = -0.5 * alpha_l[i]                       # neighbour above
c[i] = -0.5 * alpha_r[i]                       # neighbour below
b[i] =  1.0 + 0.5*(alpha_l[i] + alpha_r[i])    # the layer itself
d[i] =  0.5*alpha_l[i]*left \
        + (1 - 0.5*(alpha_l[i]+alpha_r[i]))*T_prev[i] \
        + 0.5*alpha_r[i]*right                 # the known part
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
Read \texttt{alpha} as ``how much heat can cross between these layers in
one step, relative to how much the layer can hold'' --- big \texttt{alpha}
means strongly coupled. The \texttt{a,b,c} lines hold the unknown new
temperatures (the \texttt{0.5} is the Crank--Nicolson averaging);
\texttt{d} packages everything already known from the current
temperatures \texttt{T\_prev}. Doing this for every layer gives a system
of linked equations to solve --- next.
\end{codebox}

\section{Step 3 --- solving the layer equations fast}
Because each layer touches only its two immediate neighbours, the linked
equations form a special, easy shape called \textbf{tridiagonal} (only
the diagonal and its two neighbours are non-zero). There is a classic,
very fast recipe --- the \textbf{Thomas algorithm} --- that sweeps down
once and back up once, instead of the slow general method. For hundreds
of layers, every hour of a multi-year simulation, this speed matters.
\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def _thomas(a, b, c, d):           # solve the tridiagonal system A x = d
    n = b.size
    cp = np.empty(n); dp = np.empty(n); x = np.empty(n)
    cp[0] = c[0]/b[0];  dp[0] = d[0]/b[0]
    for i in range(1, n):                      # forward sweep
        m = b[i] - a[i]*cp[i-1]
        cp[i] = c[i]/m if i < n-1 else 0.0
        dp[i] = (d[i] - a[i]*dp[i-1]) / m
    x[n-1] = dp[n-1]
    for i in range(n-2, -1, -1):               # back substitution
        x[i] = dp[i] - cp[i]*x[i+1]
    return x
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
The first loop sweeps surface-to-bottom, eliminating each layer's
dependence on the one above (\texttt{cp},\texttt{dp} are cleaned-up
coefficients). The second loop sweeps back up, reading off each layer's
new temperature \texttt{x} from the one below. The output \texttt{x} is
the temperature of every layer after this time step.
\end{codebox}

\section{Step 4 --- the surface: balancing sunlight and glow}
\subsection{Physics: the surface energy balance}
The top layer is special: its temperature is whatever makes energy
balance at every instant --- sunlight absorbed must equal infrared light
radiated away \emph{plus} heat conducted downward into the soil:
\begin{mathbox}
\[
\underbrace{(1-A)\,F_\odot}_{\text{sunlight absorbed}}
\;-\;
\underbrace{\varepsilon\,\sigma\,T_s^4}_{\text{infrared glow out}}
\;-\;
\underbrace{K\,\dfrac{T_s - T_\text{below}}{\Delta z/2}}_{\text{conducted downward}}
= 0
\]
\textbf{$A$} albedo (fraction of sunlight reflected, so $1-A$ is
absorbed, $\approx0.87$ here); \textbf{$F_\odot$} incoming sunlight;
\textbf{$\varepsilon$} emissivity ($\approx0.95$); \textbf{$\sigma$} the
Stefan--Boltzmann constant; \textbf{$T_s$} the surface temperature we
solve for. The glow grows as $T_s^4$ --- very steeply --- which is why
this cannot be solved by simple algebra.
\end{mathbox}
At lunar noon this balance gives $T_s\approx390\,$K (a scorching
$117\,^\circ$C); deep in the night, with no sunlight, it drops below
$100\,$K.

\subsection{Method: Newton's method finds $T_s$}
Because $T_s$ appears as $T_s^4$, there is no tidy formula. So the code
\emph{guesses, checks the imbalance, and corrects} --- repeatedly, using
\textbf{Newton's method}, which uses the slope of the imbalance to make
each correction. It converges in a handful of tries.
\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
def surface_energy_balance_residual(T_s, insolation, albedo, emissivity,
                                    K_surf, dz_surf, T_subsurf):
    radiative_in  = (1.0 - albedo) * insolation         # sunlight absorbed
    radiative_out = emissivity * SIGMA_SB * T_s**4       # infrared glow
    conductive    = K_surf * (T_s - T_subsurf)/(0.5*dz_surf)  # into soil
    return radiative_in - radiative_out - conductive    # the imbalance
\end{lstlisting}
\codefile{lunar/solver.py (Newton iteration, trimmed)}
\begin{lstlisting}
for _ in range(max_iter):
    R  = surface_energy_balance_residual(T_s, ...)      # current imbalance
    dR = -4.0*emissivity*SIGMA_SB*T_s**3 - 2.0*K_surf/dz_surf  # its slope
    T_s_new = T_s - R/dR                                # Newton correction
    if abs(T_s_new - T_s) < tol:
        return T_s_new                                  # converged
    T_s = T_s_new
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
The first function returns the energy imbalance \texttt{R} for a trial
surface temperature; zero means balanced. Newton's update
\texttt{T\_s\_new = T\_s - R/dR} nudges the guess toward balance using the
imbalance's slope \texttt{dR}; when the nudge becomes tiny, the surface
temperature for this instant is found.
\end{codebox}

\section{Step 5 --- the bottom: the geothermal flux}
At the bottom we impose the steady interior heat: a fixed upward flux
$Q_b$. This is what makes the deep temperature climb with depth at all ---
the column is fed from below, not sealed off (sealing it, a zero-flux
bottom, was a conceptual error this project explicitly avoids). In the
time-step assembly the bottom layer simply receives an extra dollop of
heat each step:
\codefile{lunar/solver.py}
\begin{lstlisting}
d[-1] += dt * inputs.Q_b / cap[-1]    # steady heat into the bottom layer
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
\texttt{d[-1]} is the bottom layer's known right-hand side; adding
\texttt{dt*Q\_b/cap[-1]} injects the geothermal heat ($Q_b$ over a time
\texttt{dt}, divided by the layer's heat capacity) every step. That one
line is the entire bottom boundary condition.
\end{codebox}

\section{Step 6 --- settling to equilibrium}\label{sec:equilibrium}
\subsection{Physics: why settling is slow}
With the recipe above the computer can march the lunar day forward step
by step. But to get the \emph{settled} repeating state, it must run until
the column forgets wherever it started. The time to forget scales as
$\tau\sim L^2/\kappa$ (depth squared, over diffusivity): the surface skin
($L\sim$ a few cm) forgets in a few lunar days, but the full 5\,m column
takes the equivalent of \emph{roughly a thousand} lunar days. Marching
that long for every trial $K_d$ would be hopelessly slow.

\subsection{Method and code: the shortcut}
The fix uses the steady-state fact from Step~5: once settled, the same
heat must flow through every layer, which fixes the entire deep profile
directly --- no waiting. The \textbf{equilibrium solver} alternates a
short march (to settle the fast skin) with a direct reconstruction of the
deep part, repeating until nothing changes.
\codefile{lunar/equilibrium.py (the outer loop, trimmed)}
\begin{lstlisting}
for it in range(1, max_outer + 1):
    out = solve_pixel(... n_lunations_spinup=n_inner ...)  # short march
    T_mean = out.T.mean(axis=1)                            # cycle-average
    anchor = T_mean[i0]                                    # temp at 0.55 m
    drift  = abs(anchor - anchor_prev)
    # rebuild the deep profile from steady-state heat balance:
    T_init = _reconstruct_subskin(T_mean, z, i0, Q_b, K_func, u_rect)
    if drift < anchor_tol_K and it >= 2:
        break                                              # converged
    anchor_prev = anchor
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{codebox}
Each pass does a short simulation (\texttt{solve\_pixel}), reads the
settled temperature at an anchor depth (0.55\,m), then rebuilds the deep
profile directly from the heat-balance law
(\texttt{\_reconstruct\_subskin}). When the anchor stops moving between
passes (\texttt{drift < anchor\_tol\_K}), the true settled state has been
reached --- in a handful of passes, at the cost of the old short run
rather than a thousand-day march.
\end{codebox}
\begin{keybox}
Solving the model $=$ grid (Step 1) $+$ time-stepping each layer
(Steps 2--3) $+$ the two boundary rules (Steps 4--5) $+$ settling to
equilibrium (Step 6). Those pieces are the entire forward model. Why
settling matters so much gets its own section (\S\ref{sec:bugfix}).
\end{keybox}

\section{The Apollo data and how it is cleaned}
On Apollo 15 (1971) and 17 (1972), astronauts lowered thermometers more
than two metres down (Figure~\ref{fig:probe}); the records were restored
from the original 1970s tapes in 2018. Two honest housekeeping steps turn
the raw multi-year wiggling records into clean numbers.

\textbf{1. The borestem cut.} The fibreglass stem that holds the
thermometers leaks a little surface heat downward, contaminating the
shallowest sensors. Rather than guess a correction, we discard
\emph{every} sensor above 80\,cm before looking at any answer.

\textbf{2. Stability windows.} Each record starts with disturbances (the
drilling, equipment glitches, slow drift) and only later settles. For
each sensor the code automatically scans for the longest flat stretch at
the end --- the part whose temperature trend is statistically
indistinguishable from a flat line (a slope under
$0.08\,$K\,per\,year) --- and averages it into one ``equilibrium''
temperature. That single number per sensor is what the model is compared
against.

After cleaning: 7 trustworthy points at Apollo 15, 16 at Apollo 17 ---
23 numbers in total. The whole study extracts the most defensible
conclusion from those 23 numbers.

\section{The retrieval: guess, simulate, compare}
We cannot measure $K_d$ directly, so we reverse the problem
(Figure~\ref{fig:retrieval}a): guess a $K_d$, simulate the temperatures
it produces, score the mismatch, keep the best.
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_primer_retrieval.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Extracting a number.} (a) The guess--simulate--compare
loop. (b) The real result: mismatch (RMSE) versus guessed $K_d$ forms a
bowl whose bottom is the best fit --- Apollo 15 near 4.6, Apollo 17 near
8.1; dashed line is the single global value (3.4).}
\label{fig:retrieval}
\end{figure}
\begin{mathbox}
\[
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{N}\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\big(T^{\text{model}}_i - T^{\text{data}}_i\big)^2}
\]
For each of $N$ sensors take the model$-$data gap, square it (big misses
count more, sign ignored), average, square-root back to degrees. Zero is
perfect. The lowest-RMSE $K_d$ is the answer, $K_d^{*}$.
\end{mathbox}
\begin{keybox}
The retrieved conductivity is the bottom of the mismatch bowl: 4.6 at
Apollo 15, 8.1 at Apollo 17 --- both above the global 3.4, and different
from each other.
\end{keybox}

\section{Uncertainty I: the bootstrap}
With only 7 or 16 points, how sure are we? We cannot redo Apollo, but we
can \emph{resample} our own data: treat the sensors as a bag of tickets
and draw a new set \emph{with replacement} --- some sensors picked twice,
some left out (Figure~\ref{fig:bootstrap}). Each fake set gives a
slightly different $K_d$; doing this 1{,}500 times, the spread of answers
is the uncertainty.
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_bootstrap.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The bootstrap, from scratch.} (a) Your real sensors.
(b) Many resampled sets (repeats allowed, some left out); each yields its
own $K_d$. (c) The spread of those answers is the error bar --- 95\% of
Apollo 15's resamples fall in [4.1, 7.5].}
\label{fig:bootstrap}
\end{figure}
\begin{plainbox}
Shuffling your own sample mimics the spread you would get from repeating
the whole experiment --- letting a tiny dataset honestly report its own
reliability, with no assumptions about bell curves.
\end{plainbox}
\begin{keybox}
Bootstrap $=$ measure uncertainty by reshuffling your own data many times
and watching the answer jump. Small jumps mean confident. The two sites'
difference stays positive in $\sim$99\% of reshuffles --- that is what
``statistically significant'' means here.
\end{keybox}

\section{Uncertainty II: Bayesian inference and MCMC}
A complementary method lets us fold in prior knowledge of the heat flux
$Q_b$. \textbf{Bayesian inference} combines the \textbf{prior} (what we
believed before) and the \textbf{likelihood} (what the data say) into the
\textbf{posterior} (updated belief), sharper than either
(Figure~\ref{fig:mcmc}a).
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_mcmc.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Bayesian updating and the ridge.} (a) Prior $\times$
likelihood $=$ posterior. (b) Our 2-D landscape: plausibility is high all
along a diagonal \emph{ridge} because the data fix the \emph{ratio}
$Q_b/K_d$, not each separately. Dots are MCMC ``footprints''; the star is
the published value.}
\label{fig:mcmc}
\end{figure}
\begin{mathbox}
\[
\underbrace{P(\text{answer}\mid\text{data})}_{\text{posterior}}
\;\propto\;
\underbrace{P(\text{data}\mid\text{answer})}_{\text{likelihood}}
\;\times\;
\underbrace{P(\text{answer})}_{\text{prior}}
\]
Read $P(A\mid B)$ as ``how plausible $A$ is, given $B$.'' The posterior
is the likelihood times the prior; ``$\propto$'' means we only need the
shape, then normalise.
\end{mathbox}
With two unknowns ($K_d$ and $Q_b$) the posterior is a landscape we
cannot write down, so \textbf{Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)} explores
it with random ``walkers'' that wander toward more-plausible spots,
leaving footprints whose density maps the answer --- no formula needed.
It reveals a long diagonal \textbf{ridge} (Figure~\ref{fig:mcmc}b): one
borehole fixes only the gradient, i.e.\ the ratio $Q_b/K_d$ --- halve
both and nothing changes. This is why the absolute $K_d$ depends on the
assumed $Q_b$, while the \emph{difference} between sites is sturdier.
\begin{keybox}
Bayesian/MCMC $=$ explore every answer consistent with data and prior
knowledge using random walkers. For us it confirms the bootstrap and
exposes the $K_d$--$Q_b$ ridge.
\end{keybox}

\section{Choosing between explanations: the AICc}
Two stories compete: ``different conductivity per site'' versus ``same
conductivity, different heat flux.'' We cannot just pick whichever fits
closer, because a model with more free knobs can \emph{always} fit
better, even by chasing noise --- \textbf{overfitting}
(Figure~\ref{fig:aicc}a).
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_book_aicc.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Overfitting and the AICc.} (a) Too simple misses the
trend; too complex chases noise; the middle is right. (b) The AICc adds a
penalty for extra knobs and scores our three real models: M1 (per-site
$K_d$) wins.}
\label{fig:aicc}
\end{figure}
The \textbf{Akaike Information Criterion} (``c'' for a small-sample
correction) scores each model as its misfit \emph{plus a penalty per free
parameter}; lowest wins --- good fit without needless complexity.
\begin{mathbox}
\[
\mathrm{AICc} =
\underbrace{N\ln(\text{misfit}/N)}_{\text{rewards a good fit}}
+ \underbrace{2k}_{\text{penalty per knob}}
+ \underbrace{\tfrac{2k(k+1)}{N-k-1}}_{\text{small-sample fix}}
\]
\textbf{$N$} data points, \textbf{$k$} free parameters. Only differences
between models matter: a gap $\gtrsim2$ is meaningful, $\gtrsim10$
decisive.
\end{mathbox}
Scoring our three candidate models, the ``different $K_d$ per site''
model (M1) wins; the ``same $K_d$'' alternatives score worse even though
they were allowed to fit --- the data prefer genuinely different
conductivities.
\begin{keybox}
AICc $=$ a scoreboard rewarding fit but fining complexity. It picks the
per-site-conductivity explanation over one-size-fits-all.
\end{keybox}

\section{The mistake we found and fixed}\label{sec:bugfix}
\subsection{The bug}
To start a simulation the computer needs an initial deep temperature. The
old code typed one in (250\,K for Apollo 15, 255\,K for Apollo 17) and
ran only 30 lunar days --- far short of the $\sim$1000 needed to settle.
So the deep temperature was mostly the typed-in guess, and the
conductivity read from it was partly an echo of that guess. We proved it
(Figure~\ref{fig:anchorfix}a): three starting guesses gave three
different deep profiles. An answer that moves with an arbitrary guess is
not a measurement.
\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_primer_anchorfix.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The bug and the fix} (real runs, Apollo 15). (a) Old
method: starting from 245/250/255\,K, the deep profile fans out --- the
guess leaks in. (b) New method: all three land on one curve through the
data. The answer no longer depends on the guess.}
\label{fig:anchorfix}
\end{figure}
\subsection{The fix and the check}
The equilibrium solver of \S\ref{sec:equilibrium} computes the settled
state directly. We verified it: starting from 240 vs 260\,K now agrees to
within $0.02\,$K, and a long check-run does not drift
(Figure~\ref{fig:anchorfix}b). We did \emph{not} just ``run longer'' ---
we used the heat-balance shortcut to jump to the answer.
\subsection{What changed}
\textbf{Apollo 15: $4.86\to4.58$} --- barely moved (its guess was close
by luck). \textbf{Apollo 17: $11.23\to8.12$} --- dropped a lot (its guess
was too warm and had inflated the value).
\begin{keybox}
The fix made the science \emph{stronger}: the site difference became
statistically significant, Apollo 17's value moved into the range other
evidence expects, and an independent model that used to contradict it now
agrees. When a fix makes independent checks line up, it is the right fix.
\end{keybox}

\section{Honest limits and the bottom line}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Absolute values lean on $Q_b$.} The data fix the ratio
      $Q_b/K_d$ (the ridge), so revising the heat flux shifts $K_d$
      proportionally. The site \emph{difference} is far more robust.
\item \textbf{Only two boreholes exist.} Two points cannot map the whole
      Moon --- only show these two differ. The rest needs new missions.
\item \textbf{Apollo 17 is systematics-sensitive.} Its error bar is
      tight, but its accuracy depends on a few inputs, reported openly in
      the paper's error budget.
\end{enumerate}
\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Rock-solid:} the two sites need different conductivities, Apollo
17 the more conductive. \textbf{Conditional:} the exact numbers, which
depend on the assumed heat flux and model form. The paper says, clearly,
which is which --- and that honesty is what makes it credible.
\end{keybox}

\section{Glossary of every term}
{\small
\begin{multicols}{2}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt,style=nextline,font=\bfseries,itemsep=2pt]
\item[Conduction] Heat spreading through solid material, atom to atom.
\item[Temperature / heat] Average atomic jiggle / the energy of jiggling
  on the move.
\item[Conductivity ($K$, $K_d$)] How easily heat passes; $K_d$ is the
  deep settled value --- our target. Small $=$ good insulator.
\item[Heat flux ($q$, $Q_b$)] Heat crossing one m$^2$ per second; $Q_b$
  is the steady flux from the Moon's interior.
\item[Gradient] Temperature change per metre of depth.
\item[Fourier's law] Flux $=$ conductivity $\times$ gradient.
\item[Heat equation] Temperature changes where more heat flows in than
  out.
\item[Density ($\rho$) / specific heat ($c_p$)] Mass per volume / energy
  to warm 1\,kg by 1\,K.
\item[Albedo ($A$) / emissivity ($\varepsilon$)] Fraction of sunlight
  reflected / efficiency of infrared glow.
\item[Skin depth] How far the day--night swing reaches (a few cm).
\item[Steady state] Settled condition, unchanging day to day.
\item[Boundary conditions] Rules at the column ends: surface radiation
  balance; fixed flux $Q_b$ at the bottom.
\item[Grid / discretisation] Splitting the column into layers and time
  into steps for the computer.
\item[Finite difference] Replacing a calculus rate-of-change with a
  difference between neighbours.
\item[Crank--Nicolson] A stable time-step scheme averaging now and next.
\item[Tridiagonal / Thomas] The simple shape of the layer equations, and
  the fast recipe that solves them.
\item[Harmonic mean] The correct way to average conductivity at a layer
  boundary (worse insulator dominates).
\item[Newton's method] Guess-and-correct using slope, for the surface
  temperature.
\item[Borestem cut] Discarding sensors above 80\,cm (stem contamination).
\item[Stability window] The settled tail of a sensor's record that is
  averaged into one temperature.
\item[Retrieval] Guessing $K_d$, simulating, keeping the best match.
\item[RMSE] One number for the typical model--data gap, in degrees.
\item[Bootstrap] Resampling your own data to measure uncertainty.
\item[Bayesian / prior / likelihood / posterior] Updating belief: before
  / data / after.
\item[MCMC] Random walkers mapping all plausible answers.
\item[Degeneracy / ridge] When data fix only a combination ($Q_b/K_d$),
  not each unknown.
\item[Overfitting / AICc] Fitting noise / a score that fines complexity.
\item[HFE, A15/A17] Heat-Flow Experiment; the Apollo 15 and 17 sites.
\end{description}
