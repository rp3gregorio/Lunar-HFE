\noindent\textit{Purpose.} This note lays out, end to end, what we did and why
it is defensible: how the two published conductivity models are built and why
the literature uses a single ``global'' $K_d$; how we instead retrieve $K_d$
\emph{separately} at each borehole; what changed between the old and new
steady-state solver and why the two give the same answer; a physical reading of
why the two sites differ; and the role of the basal heat flux $Q_b$. (The
statistical machinery --- bootstrap intervals, model selection --- is deferred;
here the focus is the physics and the method.)

% =====================================================================
\section{The goal and the two sites}
% =====================================================================
The deep regolith thermal conductivity $K_d$ sets how steeply temperature rises
with depth and how much of the Moon's interior heat reaches the surface. The
Apollo Heat-Flow Experiment (HFE) left buried temperature sensors at two very
different places (\figref{fig:map}): \textbf{Apollo~15} at Hadley Rille on the
Imbrium mare/Apennine boundary, and \textbf{Apollo~17} in the Taurus--Littrow
valley among highland massifs and dark pyroclastic mantle. The published thermal
models use \emph{one} global $K_d$ for the whole Moon. Our question is simple:
\emph{what is $K_d$ at each of these boreholes, separately?}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/fig_context_map.pdf}
\caption{The two heat-flow boreholes sit in geologically distinct terrains ---
a first hint that a single global conductivity may not fit both.}
\label{fig:map}
\end{figure}

\section{The two published conductivity models (and how to reproduce them)}
% =====================================================================
Both models give the regolith conductivity $K$ as a function of temperature $T$
and depth $z$. Reproducing them is just evaluating the formulas below; our code
does exactly this in \code{lunar/properties.py}.

\subsection{Hayne et al.\ (2017): the H-parameter model}
The regolith is loose and porous at the surface and compacts with depth, so the
solid (contact) conductivity rises from a surface value $K_s$ to a deep value
$K_d$ over an $e$-folding depth $H$:
\begin{equation}
K_c(z) = K_d - (K_d - K_s)\,e^{-z/H}.
\label{eq:Kc}
\end{equation}
Radiation across pore spaces then adds a strongly temperature-dependent term:
\begin{equation}
K(T,z) = K_c(z)\,\Bigl[\,1 + \chi\,(T/T_{\mathrm{ref}})^{3}\,\Bigr],
\qquad T_{\mathrm{ref}}=350\,\mathrm{K}.
\label{eq:Khayne}
\end{equation}
The bulk density follows the same compaction law,
$\rho(z)=\rho_d-(\rho_d-\rho_s)e^{-z/H}$. The cited parameters are:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Symbol & Value & Meaning \\
\midrule
$K_s$ & $0.74\ \kdunit$ & surface contact conductivity \\
$K_d$ & $3.4\ \kdunit$ \textbf{(global)} & \textbf{deep contact conductivity --- the value we refine} \\
$H$   & $0.06\,\mathrm{m}$ & compaction $e$-folding depth \\
$\chi$ & $2.7$ & radiative-term strength \\
$T_{\mathrm{ref}}$ & $350\,\mathrm{K}$ & radiative normalisation \\
$\rho_s,\rho_d$ & $1100,\,1800\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$ & surface / deep bulk density \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Why the literature value is $K_d=3.4$.} Hayne et al.\ fit
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Kc}--\eqref{eq:Khayne} to the \emph{globally averaged} diurnal
surface-temperature curves measured from orbit by the Diviner radiometer. A
single $(K_s,K_d,H,\chi)$ best reproduced the global day--night behaviour, and
$K_d=3.4\ \kdunit$ is that global-average deep asymptote. It was never meant to
be site-specific --- which is exactly the gap we fill. \figref{fig:models}(a)
shows where that asymptote sits and where our per-site values move it.

\subsection{Mart\'inez \& Siegler (2021): a density-based model}
This model writes the conductivity directly in terms of bulk density $\rho$,
splitting it into a contact (phonon) term and a radiative term:
\begin{equation}
K(T,\rho) = (A_1\rho + A_2)\,k_{\mathrm{am}}(T) \;+\; (B_1\rho + B_2)\,T^{3},
\label{eq:Kmartinez}
\end{equation}
where $k_{\mathrm{am}}(T)$ is the Woods--Robinson et al.\ (2019) amorphous-solid
polynomial and $A_{1,2},B_{1,2}$ are fitted constants. The two models use
different physics for the same quantity; \figref{fig:models}(b) shows they
predict comparable total conductivity and the same steep rise with temperature.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_models.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Reproducing both models.} (a)~Hayne contact conductivity
$K_c(z)$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:Kc}: it climbs from $K_s$ at the surface to its deep
asymptote $K_d$. The published \emph{global} value is $3.4$; our per-site
retrievals push it to $4.58$ (A15) and $8.12$ (A17). (b)~Full conductivity vs.\
temperature at $1$\,m, Eq.~\eqref{eq:Khayne}, for Hayne and Mart\'inez --- the
radiative $\propto T^3$ term roughly doubles $K$ by lunar midday. Both panels
are evaluated directly from the model code.}
\label{fig:models}
\end{figure}

\section{Our method: retrieving \texorpdfstring{$K_d$}{Kd} per site}
% =====================================================================
\subsection{The forward model}
We solve the 1-D heat equation in the regolith column,
\begin{equation}
\rho(z)\,c_p(T)\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}
   = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\!\left(K(T,z)\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}\right),
\label{eq:heat}
\end{equation}
on a grid that is millimetre-fine at the top (to resolve the daily thermal wave)
and reaches $5$\,m. The surface obeys a non-linear radiative balance,
$(1-A)\,S(t) = \varepsilon\sigma T_s^4 + K\,\partial_z T|_0$, and the bottom
admits the geothermal flux, $K\,\partial_z T = Q_b$. Driven by the periodic Sun,
the column settles to a periodic steady state; that settled profile is what we
compare to the sensors.

\subsection{The retrieval}
We hold the Hayne functional form fixed and treat $K_d$ as the single unknown.
For each trial $K_d$ we solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:heat} to steady state and measure the
misfit to the deep sensors,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RMSE}(K_d) = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{N}\textstyle\sum_i
   \bigl[\,\Tm(z_i;K_d) - T^{\mathrm{obs}}_i\,\bigr]^2 },
\qquad
K_d^{*} = \arg\min_{K_d}\ \mathrm{RMSE}(K_d).
\end{equation}
The misfit curve has a clean single minimum at each site (\figref{fig:sweep}),
and that minimum is the retrieved $K_d^{*}$.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.76\linewidth]{figures/fig_kd_sweep.pdf}
\caption{The retrieval, visualised: deep-sensor RMSE vs.\ trial $K_d$. Each site
has a well-defined minimum, and Apollo~17's sits at a clearly higher
conductivity than Apollo~15's.}
\label{fig:sweep}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Result}
The retrieval gives
\[
K_{d,\mathrm{A15}}^{*} = 4.58\ \kdunit,\qquad
K_{d,\mathrm{A17}}^{*} = 8.12\ \kdunit,
\]
i.e.\ Apollo~17 is about twice as conductive as Apollo~15. With these per-site
values the modelled deep temperatures track the data far better than the single
global $K_d$ does (\figref{fig:profiles}); at Apollo~17 the misfit is roughly
halved. (Confidence intervals and significance are quantified separately by
bootstrap --- deferred here.)

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.76\linewidth]{figures/fig_thermal_profiles.pdf}
\caption{Settled mean temperature vs.\ depth using each site's \emph{retrieved}
$K_d^{*}$, against the deep-sensor measurements. The per-site fit threads the
data better than the global value can.}
\label{fig:profiles}
\end{figure}

\section{Old vs.\ new: how we reach the steady state}
% =====================================================================
Everything above rests on ``solve to steady state.'' That step is where the old
code went wrong, and where the new method earns its trust.

\subsection{What ``steady state'' requires}
The periodic steady state is pinned by two facts: the surface skin repeats each
lunation, and --- below the skin, with no internal heat sources --- the same
flux passes through every layer and equals the bottom flux $Q_b$. That fixes the
deep gradient:
\begin{equation}
K(\Tm,z)\,\frac{d\Tm}{dz}=Q_b
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\frac{d\Tm}{dz}=\frac{Q_b}{K(\Tm,z)} .
\label{eq:closure}
\end{equation}
The deep profile is therefore \emph{not} free; energy conservation fixes its
slope once the temperature at one depth is known.

\subsection{The old method, and its hidden bias (flag F1)}
The old approach integrated Eq.~\eqref{eq:heat} forward for a fixed, short
spin-up ($\sim$30 lunations). But the full column relaxes on a diffusive
timescale of order $10^3$ lunations, so at sensor depth the profile still
\emph{remembered the temperature it was started from}. That starting guess then
leaked into $K_d$ as a hidden parameter. The trap: the usual ``did $\Tm$ change
by $<0.01$\,K between lunations?'' check passes \emph{long before} the column is
settled, so the answer looks converged while it is still drifting.

\subsection{How the old spin-up actually runs --- step by step}
It helps to watch what the model literally does. \figref{fig:film}
(\textbf{animated in \code{docs/justification/spinup.gif}} --- play it) shows one
brute-force spin-up from start to finish.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Start from a guess.} We fill the whole $5$\,m column with one
temperature --- here a flat $240$\,K (the green line, first panel). It is
deliberately wrong; the point is to watch the model try to correct it.
\item \textbf{Step through one lunar day.} ``One lunation'' is a full day--night
cycle ($\sim$29.5 Earth days), which we march through in hourly steps. The Sun
heats the surface by day and it radiates heat away by night, so the top few
centimetres swing through a huge range (the shaded band). But that daily wave
dies out within $\sim$10--20\,cm --- below that, the rock barely feels day or
night.
\item \textbf{Heat seeps downward --- slowly.} What reaches the deep regolith is
not the daily wave but its \emph{average}, which conducts a little deeper each
lunation. It is like warming a thick wall from one face: the far side responds
only after a long time. After \textbf{30 lunations} (\figref{fig:film}, the
highlighted panel) only the top $\sim$0.5\,m has adjusted --- the regolith from
$\sim$1 to $5$\,m is still near the wrong $240$\,K start, and the steady deep
slope (set by $Q_b$) has not formed.
\item \textbf{Why so slow?} The time for heat to diffuse to depth $z$ grows like
$z^{2}$, so reaching $5$\,m takes of order $10^{3}$ lunations --- about thirty
times longer than the old $30$-lunation spin-up.
\item \textbf{The damage.} The HFE sensors sit at $0.8$--$2.4$\,m --- exactly the
region that has \emph{not} settled at 30 lunations. The old code read its $K_d$
off that half-baked profile, so the arbitrary starting temperature leaked
straight into the answer (flag~F1).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/spinup_filmstrip.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{What ``brute force'' actually is.} Snapshots of one spin-up
(green $=$ current profile; dashed $=$ the true steady state it is heading for;
shaded $=$ the daily temperature swing). At 0 lunations the column is at the
flat guess; by 30 lunations only the top has moved while the deep part still sits
near the start; it takes hundreds of lunations to reach the target. The
``brute-force line'' in the other figures is just this green curve once it has
finally stopped moving.}
\label{fig:film}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The new method: a flux-anchored shortcut}
The new solver (\code{lunar/equilibrium.py}) does not wait for diffusion to
discover Eq.~\eqref{eq:closure} --- it \emph{imposes} it, alternating two moves
(\figref{fig:newfilm}; animated in \code{docs/justification/newmethod.gif}):
\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.6em,itemsep=2pt]
\item[\textnormal{\textbf{Step A --- settle the skin.}}] A \emph{short} spin-up
(a few lunations) settles only the fast surface skin and reads the cycle-mean
``anchor'' temperature just below it. The deep column is left alone.
\item[\textnormal{\textbf{Step B --- reconstruct the deep profile.}}] From that
anchor, integrate $d\Tm/dz=Q_b/K$ straight down. This \emph{snaps} the whole deep
profile onto the correct steady slope in one shot --- no waiting for heat to
diffuse there. (\code{docs/justification/reconstruct.gif} animates exactly this:
start at the anchor and walk downward; at every depth the slope is forced to be
$Q_b/K$, so the deep profile draws itself.)
\end{description}
Repeat A--B until the anchor stops moving. The decisive contrast: after only a
few lunations the old method's deep regolith has \emph{not moved at all}
(\figref{fig:film}), whereas the new method's first reconstruct already puts the
deep profile on the right gradient (\figref{fig:newfilm}). In total the new
method uses $\sim$150 lunations of computation ($\sim$9\,s, about a dozen short
A--B rounds), versus the $\sim$1000+ lunations the old method must crawl
through --- like computing a tank's steady level from ``inflow $=$ outflow''
instead of waiting for the sloshing to stop.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/newmethod_filmstrip.pdf}
\caption{The new method, step by step (compare \figref{fig:film}). A short
spin-up settles the skin (Step A); the reconstruct move then integrates
$d\Tm/dz=Q_b/K$ to snap the deep profile onto the steady gradient (Step B); a
dozen short rounds refine it to the target. It reaches the \emph{same} profile
as brute force in a tiny fraction of the work.}
\label{fig:newfilm}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{What is the ``target'' in these figures?} The dashed line in
\figref{fig:film} and \figref{fig:newfilm} is the \emph{true periodic steady
state} --- the one profile that satisfies both defining conditions
(periodic skin $+$ flux closure, \S4.1). We draw it as the new method's
converged output, but it is not an arbitrary choice: it is exactly the profile
the old brute-force method crawls onto when run to completion. \figref{fig:conv}
and \figref{fig:overlay} show that independently --- brute force, started from
two different temperatures and run for thousands of lunations, lands on this
same line. So ``target'' $=$ ``what both methods agree on,'' computed quickly by
one and confirmed by the other.

\subsection{Do they agree? Yes --- run the lunations and watch}
The cleanest demonstration is to take the \emph{old} method and simply give it
more lunations: as the spin-up lengthens, its profile marches monotonically onto
the new method's answer (\figref{fig:conv}). Starting from two very different
temperatures, $240$\,K and $260$\,K, both collapse onto the same curve
(\figref{fig:overlay}). They must: both enforce the same two conditions, and the
steady state satisfying them is unique --- the shortcut just gets there in
$\sim$9\,s instead of the $\sim$3000 lunations ($\sim$4\,min) brute force needs.

\begin{takeaway}
The new method is not a different model --- it reaches the \emph{same} periodic
steady state, certified by two diagnostics it returns on every solve
(guess-independence $\le 0.03$\,K, and the deep flux closing on $Q_b$ to $<2\%$;
\figref{fig:cert}), and confirmed by brute force converging onto it. It removes
the F1 bias not by running longer but by not needing to.
\end{takeaway}

\section{Why might the two sites differ?}
% =====================================================================
A factor-of-two conductivity contrast is large but physically reasonable: the
two boreholes sample different regolith.

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Apollo 15} & \textbf{Apollo 17} \\
\midrule
Setting & Hadley Rille (mare/front) & Taurus--Littrow valley \\
Latitude & $26.1^\circ$N & $20.2^\circ$N \\
Bond albedo & 0.131 & 0.137 \\
Deepest sensor & $1.39$\,m & $2.34$\,m \\
Basal flux $Q_b$ (held fixed) & $21\ \mathrm{mW\,m^{-2}}$ & $15\ \mathrm{mW\,m^{-2}}$ \\
\textbf{Retrieved $K_d^{*}$} & $\mathbf{4.58}$ & $\mathbf{8.12}\ \kdunit$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
Heat moves through dry regolith mainly by grain-to-grain contact, so a higher
$K_d$ points to a denser, more compacted, or more rock-rich deep regolith.
Plausible contributors at Apollo~17:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Material and compaction.} Taurus--Littrow mixes highland massif
debris with dense pyroclastic (dark-mantle) material; Hadley samples mare-basalt
regolith. Different parent material and packing change grain contact and bulk
density.
\item \textbf{Independent support.} Re-running the retrieval with the
\emph{density}-based Mart\'inez model (Eq.~\eqref{eq:Kmartinez}) prefers a higher
effective deep density at Apollo~17 --- the same contrast seen through a model
whose free lever is density rather than $K_d$. Two independent descriptions
point the same way.
\item \textbf{Rock abundance / gardening.} More buried fragments, or a more
mature and sintered column, would also raise the effective conductivity.
\end{itemize}

\begin{caveat}
We hold $Q_b$ fixed (next section), so part of the inferred contrast is, in
principle, exchangeable with a flux difference. A formal model comparison
(deferred) favours a \emph{conductivity} contrast over a flux contrast, but the
honest framing is: \emph{Apollo~17's regolith conducts heat about twice as
efficiently as Apollo~15's, conditional on the published basal fluxes.}
\end{caveat}

% =====================================================================
\section{The role of the basal heat flux \texorpdfstring{$Q_b$}{Qb}}
% =====================================================================
$K_d$ cannot be read off in isolation: the deep gradient is $Q_b/K$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:closure}), so $K_d$ and $Q_b$ \emph{trade off}. Assume more heat
from below and you would infer a higher conductivity to reproduce the same
temperatures. The two are entangled, and this single record cannot separate
them.

We therefore fix $Q_b$ at the published Apollo values --- $21\ \mathrm{mW\,m^{-2}}$
(A15) and $15\ \mathrm{mW\,m^{-2}}$ (A17) --- and report $K_d$ \emph{conditional}
on them. \figref{fig:qb} maps the trade-off: many $K_d$--$Q_b$ combinations along
a diagonal fit almost equally well, so the absolute $K_d$ shifts if $Q_b$ does.
Reassuringly, the \emph{direction} of the inter-site contrast (A17 $>$ A15)
persists even when $Q_b$ is allowed to vary, and $Q_b$ is one of the largest
single terms in the error budget --- a direct fingerprint of this coupling.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.64\linewidth]{figures/fig_kd_qb_posterior.pdf}
\caption{The $K_d$--$Q_b$ trade-off. Because the deep gradient is $Q_b/K$, the
two are degenerate along a diagonal; the retrieved $K_d$ is conditional on the
assumed $Q_b$. The contrast \emph{between} sites is more robust than either
absolute value.}
\label{fig:qb}
\end{figure}

\section{Why this is defensible --- the short version}
% =====================================================================
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{The models are reproduced from first formulas}
(Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Kc}--\eqref{eq:Kmartinez}, \figref{fig:models}); the global
$K_d=3.4$ is a deliberate global average we are refining, not a measured site
value.
\item \textbf{The retrieval is transparent}: one unknown, a single RMSE minimum
per site (\figref{fig:sweep}), and a visibly better fit to the data
(\figref{fig:profiles}).
\item \textbf{The engine is trustworthy}: the new solver is certified
(\figref{fig:cert}) \emph{and} independently confirmed by brute force converging
onto it as the lunations increase (\figref{fig:conv},~\ref{fig:overlay}) --- the
old hidden-spin-up bias is gone.
\item \textbf{The contrast is physically sensible} (denser/different regolith at
Apollo~17) and is echoed by an independent density-based model.
\item \textbf{We are explicit about $Q_b$}: $K_d$ is conditional on the assumed
basal flux, the trade-off is mapped, and the inter-site \emph{direction} is the
robust statement.
\end{enumerate}
